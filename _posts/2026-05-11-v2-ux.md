All software faces a basic question: how does the user tell it what to do?
Since the 1980s the dominant model has been drearily consistent: reach for the
mouse, navigate into an application's menus, and click the needed operation.
Ordinary navigational tasks are similar, minus the menus: reach, point,
scroll, click. Keyboard shortcuts for some of those operations do exist.
Nearly all users learn a handful — copy, paste, print, arrow keys — and stop
there. Some users invest real effort to learn the bindings of applications
they use heavily, but even the office expert in Excel eventually hits a
ceiling: the brain will hold only so many arbitrary CTRL-ALT combinations.

I recently completed [LoopLlama][llv2], a browser-based tool for close study
of YouTube videos. The app has menus and familiar mouse-oriented controls such
as buttons, dropdowns, and toggles. But at its core, LoopLlama has a
keyboard-first design: my priority was to be able to do everything efficiently
with simple key presses while holding a guitar and wearing a thumb pick,
without tedious pointing and clicking. Building it sharpened my convictions
about keyboard design and produced a working example of a better UX paradigm —
one with strong historical precedents but that, tragically, was ignored as our
computing culture solidified in the late 20th century.

## S1: The trouble with mice

The mouse is an instrument tuned for actions that are inherently spatial:
pointing, dragging, positioning, drawing — tasks with a direct mapping between
hand movement and cursor position.

Command input is not that. When a user reaches for the mouse to invoke a menu
operation or perform a routine navigation, nothing about that operation
requires pointing. The action is discrete: the user wants to save a file, undo
a change, or toggle a setting. The mouse arrives at the target by the same
mechanism it uses to draw a curve — slow physical travel, target acquisition,
and click — but the curve-drawing precision is wasted. You do not need a
pointing instrument to say "save." Like a caveman, you point for routine
operations only when you lack the words.

The costs are individually tiny but undeniably real in aggregate. Across a day
of routine work, the mouse traversals compound into a hefty ergonomic load.
Perhaps less obviously but more importantlay, mouse operations are not readily
scripted, chained, or automated. The keyboard, by contrast, is already where
the hands are, and its input is discrete, precise, and directly amenable to
composition in larger, automatable opererations.

The case for the keyboard as the natural command instrument is not subtle, and
the problem was recognized early in computing history. Two examples will suffice:

- [Douglas Engelbart][douglas_engelbart] led the Augmentation Research Center
  Lab in SRI International during the 1960s. In 1968, Engelbart and other ARC
  staffers presented their [oN-Line System][nls] (NLS), which included many of the
  elements of modern, interactive computing, including the computer mouse. The
  presentation was so striking to subsequent observers that be become known as
  [The Mother of All Demos][mother_demos]. Working toward the larger goal of
  making knowledge work more powerful, Engelbart and his team understood the
  division of labor sketched above. NLS paired the mouse with the keyboard:
  the mouse for spatial tasks, the keyboard for text entry and commands.[^1]
  Only the pointing half of that vision survived contact with the marketplace.
  The resulting arrangement — mouse for everything, including commands — was
  not what Engelbart had in mind.

- Later in the 1980s and 1990s, human-computer interface expert [Jef
  Raskin][jef_raskin] made the case in more rigorous terms. His work drew on
  two tools from the empirical literature. [Fitts's Law][fitts_law] provides a
  predictive model for target-acquisition time as a function of distance and
  target size. Applied to command input, it quantifies the cost of
  keyboard-to-mouse traversal on every command. The [Keystroke-Level
  Model][keystroke_model], a framework for predicting task completion time
  from its physical components, confirms the pattern: mouse operations are
  consistently and measurably slower than keyboard equivalents for discrete
  commands.

## S2: The command-line tradition and its constraints

Although the consumer market would become dominated by mouse-and-menus, an
older tradition — Unix and the command-line world — had built a culture
around keyboard primacy: application bindings, keyboard shortcuts, editors
with layered command vocabularies. Since command-line computing became
powerful before the mouse existed as a widely available device, the mouse
could, at best, augment command-line computing at the margins, but it would
never be central.

The applications running in that computing tradition faced an unavoidable
constraint: large application have large command vocabularies, but the
keyboard offers limited real estate. A typical modern keyboard has 50 keys
producing regular characters, and only 26 of them (the letters) have obvious
mnemonic potential.[^2] LoopLlama, for example, is a rich application for its
purpose, but it is tiny compared to the software that computer users spend the
most time with: word processors, spreadsheet applications, web browsers, and
many others. LoopLlama has roughly 100 key bindings to cover its operations
— already twice the keyboard's binding capacity.

The dominant answer to that constraint was keyboard expansion, along two
pathways. One was additive: function keys, a dedicated row of extra keys above
the main keyboard — provided twelve new slots available for direct binding.

The second was more powerful because it was, or at least could be,
multiplicative: modifier keys. A keyboard attached to a computer has an
intuitive default: every key emits a character. To invoke an operation rather
than emit a character, you need the hardware event (the key press) to send a
signal other than the key's default character. Typewriters already supplied a
model in the `Shift` key, which produces a different letter or a punctuation
mark. CTRL was the computing extension of that idea. In early keyboards,
holding CTRL while pressing a letter resulted in a different ASCII code than
the unmodified key press. Because of constraints of the ASCII system and the
hardware commonly available, only 32 ASCII codes were reserved for CTRL
(letters plus 6 punctuation marks). So CTRL, strictly speaking, was another
additive expansion. Later, hardware introduced in the consumer market spread
into command-line environments (Alt from the IBM PC, Cmd and Option from the
Mac).

By the end of the 1990s, a keyboard-oriented user would have not only more
keys (12 function keys, arrows, and perhaps even expansion areas for a number
keypad or various navigational keys) but also several modifiers. Those
additions faced some hardware and encoding limits too, as well as various key
reservations by the operating system. But one could fairly say the net effect
of those keyboard expansion pathways was multiplicative. Instead of having
only the 32 ASCII Ctrl code available for binding to commands, applications
running in those environments would have hundreds of slots available for
binding — roughly 800 based on my back-of-the-envelope calculations.

Although the expansion of key binding real estate was impressive in
quantitative terms, most of that terrain turned out to useless in practice,
because humans cannot remember the bindings. The letter component of a binding
can carry genuine meaning: `Cmd-p` for print, `Cmd-s` for save, `Cmd-c` for
copy. But the modifiers themselves are abstractions. Any given operation might
use `Cmd`, `Cmd-Shift`, `Cmd-Option`, or something more elaborate. Under
favorable conditions, a well-designed application could group bindings
thematically under different modifer combinations to mitigate the mnemonic
problems. In practice, the logic behind the binding schemes of many major
applications is somewhere between opaque and non-existent.

The other problem with the modifier strategy is physical. Although Shift sits
at the natural extension of the pinky's reach, the primary modifiers for
issuing commands are ergonomic disasters, involving either awkward stretches
to reach a single modifer or completely shifting the hand out of typing
position to press more than one.

In the consumer market, an unfortunate third pathway emerged as a response to
the keyboard real estate challenge: for many operations, there would be no key
binding at all. Most users learned a handful of keyboard shortcuts and stopped
there. The rest of their command vocabulary lived behind menus and long days
of pointing and clicking.

One cultural response to those constraints were calls to embrace expertise.
Such reactions sometimes took the form of specific individuals investing
sufficient effort to learn an application's incoherent list of modifier-based
bindings, drilling it into muscle memory over years. Another reaction took the
form of a wholesale critique of not so much of the mouse but of the GUI
itself, perhaps most famously in Neil Stephenson's [essay][stephenson_essay],
"In the Beginning... Was the Command Line," which constrasted the power of
command-line computing (his metaphor was Linux as a freely available tank)
against the limitations and frustrations of GUI computing (Windows as a
station wagon and Mac as a sports car, both prone to breakdowns). Stephenson
understood why so many users opted for the initial ease and reassurance of
purchasing from friendly dealer, but he upheld the virtues of the minority
that would invest the time to learn how to drive and maintain a tank.

Of course, most users will never invest that heavily in most of the software
they use, because their usage will not be substantial enough to justify the
cost. On the other hand, most people have at least one application they use
often enough to warrant such investment: the person who spends a significant
chunk of every workday in Word, Excel, or Outlook; the retiree who manages
photos in Lightroom every weekend; the teacher who assembles lecture slides in
PowerPoint. Additional expertise would pay. But if the route to expertise goes
through a thicket of modifier-based key bindings, few will make the journey.

## S3: Raskin's case against modes

Jef Raskin, one of the human-computer interface experts mentioned above, had
long been unhappy with both computing paradigms on offer. On one pole was the
expert-oriented computing tradition of Unix and the command-line. Although
powerful, this user interface provided poor visual feedback, was recall heavy,
and demanded exact syntax with no tolerance for approximation. In any case,
since it had already lost any claim on the mainstream, it was not his primary
target. Meanwhile, the GUI systems from Windows and Apple had become almost as
opaque as the command-line systems they replaced, with deep menu hierarchies
that required users to know where to look when trying to perform a task — a
mnemonic burden in a new form.

In 2000 Raskin published [The Humane Interface][humane_interface], which
consolidated and elaborated on the critiques of the prevailding computer
interfaces that he had been developing since the 1980s. The book's guiding
claims were that interfaces should prioritize alignment with human cognition
rather than computer internals and that design should aim to increase
efficiency for all users, not just experts willing to commit time to mastering
difficult systems. At least in principle, both ideas implied an empirical
orientation to observe real behavior with any proposed interface.

Both cognitive science and everyday experience show that practiced actions
become habitual, automatic: driving, typing, using a TV remote, riding a
bicyle. A central idea in Raskin's work is that good interfaces should aim to
provide a basis for automaticity to form — for users to stop consciously
deciding and simply to act. By constrast, interfaces that change from version
to version; that have different behaviors in different contexts, forcing user
to track state mentally; that have a lack of feedback or large delays between
action and response; that have high error costs (for example, no undo),
forcing users into hyper-diligence — such traits force users to act
consciously rather than automatically.

Raskin identified modes as the most systematic of these failures. A mode is a
state of the interface in which the same user action produces different
results The computing environment of the 1980s and 1990s supplied vivid
examples: the Caps Lock key, inherited from the typewriter, which silently
shifted every subsequent keystroke to uppercase; word processors that toggled
between inserting and overwriting text; image editors and presentation tools
that required the user to track which drawing tool was active; as well as one
of the most prominent text editors in the command-line world, vi, which was
built on top of a modal foundation. In each case, the same action — a
key press or a mouse click — produced different results depending on
application state the user had to remember.

In such interfaces, mode errors can occur any time the user becomes too
preoccupied or rushed with their work and forgets which mode is active before
typing or clicking. Such errors were bothersome for novice users, of course,
but Raskin's deeper critque emphasized how they worked to undermine the
automaticity upon which expertise rested. The same automatic firing that makes
experts efficient makes them particularly vulnerable to mode errors: if you
wrongly think you are in insert-mode in vim, the more letters you type, the
more likely you are to do some damage.

Raskin's prescription followed from the diagnosis: modes should be eliminated
whenever possible. Where they could not be, his preferred alternative was the
quasimode — a held key, active only while pressed. Unlike a persistent mode, a
quasimode cannot be easily forgotten: the interface's mode switch becomes
obvious, tactile.

## S4: The Canon Cat: when theory meets practice

Overview of the Cat:

  - Although Raskin had played a leading role in an early phase of the
    Macintosh project, he was disappointed by the result.

  - In 1987 he led the design of an innovative alternative, the Canon Cat,
    which provided an early example of the kind of interface that would be
    championed in The Humane Interface.

  - The design consciously rejected the then-prevelant stratagies for solving
    the computer interface challenge. To appreciate the Cat's design, it might
    be helpful to review.

    - The challenge.
      - A keyboard's primary job -- emiting regular characters -- consumes
        almost all of the keyboard's real estate right out of the gate.
      - A rudimentary keyboard with a CTRL modifier offered only 32 ASCII
        slots to allocate for invoking operations.
      - Any non-trivial application will have many more operations than that.

    - Strategies used to address it:
      - Keyboard expansion: F-keys and especially with more modifiers.
      - Surrender: put the less important operations in a menu hierarchy and
        force users to rely on inefficient pointing and clicking.
      - Embrace modes more fully, which allows the interface to repurpose keys
        in different ways, depending on the mode.

  - Because Raskin rejected the last two strategies, the Cat innovated on
    the modifier strategy. It did so in two ways.

    - Two USE FRONT keys:
      - Positioned on L/R sides of space bar.
      - The customary mods were gone.
      - In their place were USE FRONT keys.
      - Use to invoke ops printed on the front edges of various keys.
          - Examples: Copy, Calc, Print, Bold, Indent, Spell Checker, etc.
      - To invoke an operation:
        - Press and hold USE FRONT.
        - Then press the key with the desired op.

    - Backward and forward LEAP keys.
      - Positioned below space bar.
      - For use by the thumbs.
      - Ergo improvement over awkward placement of typical mod keys.
      - Used mainly for 3 kinds of operations:
        - Notice generally that the LEAP keys, like USE FRONT, were
          quasimodal: they changed the keyboard's behavior only while the user
          pressed the key.

        - Navigation:
          - Hold the LEAP for the direction of travel.
          - Type a literal search query.
          - Dynamically moves the cursor as you type.
          - To repeat the same search, the user simply had to notice that
            "Leap again" was printed on the front edges of both LEAP keys.
            - Leap once.
            - Hold USE FRONT.
            - Press LEAP again.

        - Text selection:
          - Define a selection by starting with cursor at either endpoint.
          - Leap to move the cursor to the other endpoint.
          - Press both leap keys at same time: this highlights the text.

        - Text movement:
          - Select some text.
          - Use Leap to move the entire selection.
          - Again, via the same literal search query process.

Commercial outcome:
  - Failure:
    - Sold poorly.
    - Discontinued after roughly a year.
  - Might be explained by ineffective business strategy or vageries of the
    fluid computing market at the time.
  - But my retrospective judgment is that the Cat was based on a deeply flawed
    vision for computing.
    - Those flaws are systematic, not incidental, and they cluster around
      three aspects of the design.

Limited vision:
  - The Cat does adopt the surrender strategy.
  - The GUI computer market surrendered by requiring users to mouse their way
    through menus any time they needed an operation that lacked a binding.
  - Without emphasizing the move directly, the Cat surrenders by drastically
    restricting the device's scope:
      - At least for its primary usages, it was mainly a word processing
        appliance, not a general purpose computer.
      - It had some general computing at the margins:
        - A calculator via its CALC button.
        - A lower level toolchain for coding in Forth and assembly language.
      - But it lacked support for third-party software.
    - Only a severely restricted device could rely on printed operations on
      the fronts of the keys.

The UX sour spot: navigation is poor for beginners and experts alike:

  - Ideological commitment to LEAP as the sole navigation primitive
    failed both ends of the user spectrum.

  - Beginners:

    - Blocked from the start:
      - The Cat had no arrow keys.
      - So it was a RTFM device right out of the gate.
      - The LEAP keys did operate as left/right arrows, one character per
        press. The manual calls this "creeping" and admonishes users not to
        rely on creeping as a crutch.

    - Taxed forever:
      - Once a user learned how to leap, they would forever pay the ergo tax
        of having to hold down the LEAP key while typing their search query
        string.
      - That's the unavoidable physical tax of Raskin's favored quasimode
        strategy -- the same tax paid by every modifier system.

    - Puzzled when learning less frequent operations:
      - For less frequent operations, the key combos and sequences required to
        invoke them can often seem like a convoluted finger dance, at least
        judging by my reading of the user manual, which at times felt like a
        brain teaser.

  - Intermediates and experts quickly hit a low ceiling:
    - LEAP is literal text search only
    - No regex or patterns and text objects are absent:

      - GUI word processors have long had at least basic elements of pattern
        searching (eg wildcards) as well as navigational support for text
        objects like words and paragraphs.
      - Meanwhile, command-line editors have always supported powerful
        behaviors on both fronts (more on that in a subsequent section).

      - The Cat's user manual could only offer approximate workarounds for some
        text objects via literal search:
        - Word boundaries: space.
        - Sentence: period.
        - Paragraph: 2 returns.

Theory violation: modes were smuggled into the system:

  - The wide/narrow cursor:
    - Cat cursor had two states: wide and narrow.
      - Controlled direction of text erasure and whether typing would
        insert or replace existing text.

  - Without question, this is modal behavior:
  - More strikingly, it is exactly like the mode distinctions found in the
    software that occupied the opposite end of the spectrum from Raskin on
    the question of modes: the command-line text editor, vi.

    - Vim users know whether they are in normal mode (for navigation and
      commands), insert mode (for regular typing), or replace mode (to
      replace existing text as you type) via the same signal used by the
      Cat: cursor shape.

## S5: Modes of inevitability

- Transition in: the Cat as evidence, not just cautionary tale. If modes
  couldn't be avoided in a single-domain appliance designed by their most
  determined opponent, they almost certainly can't be avoided in
  general-purpose computing.

  - The Cat's failure at the design level is not just an embarrassment for a
    particular product. It is a clue. If Raskin -- the most committed
    anti-modes theorist in the field -- could not build a real system without
    modes, that is evidence that modes are not a design flaw to be engineered
    around. They may be a structural feature of how computing works.

- Modes are everywhere

  - Applications are themselves modes. Pressing `x` in Excel does something
    different from pressing `x` in LoopLlama. Every time a user switches
    applications, they switch modes -- they just aren't labeled as such.
  - Dialog boxes, confirmation prompts, focus capture, application states:
    computing is saturated with context-dependent behavior. The question was
    never whether modes exist. It was always what properties make them
    cognitively manageable.
  - Raskin's anti-modes position, taken literally, is incoherent as an
    absolute claim. A modeless general-purpose computer is not possible; it
    would require a single flat namespace for every command in every
    application, with no context-sensitivity of any kind.

- What makes a mode good or bad: five criteria (our framework, not sourced
  from Raskin or a specific text -- derived from the argument)

  - Visibility: is the current mode indicated to the user? The user should
    never have to guess.
  - Switching cost: is the transition deliberate and low-cost? Accidental
    mode changes -- the Caps Lock problem -- are the failure case.
  - Task clarity: does the mode correspond to a task distinction the user
    already recognizes? A mode that maps to a real phase of work (navigating
    vs. entering text) costs less than one that maps to an arbitrary
    technical state.
  - Coherence: do the behaviors grouped under the mode share a logical
    identity, and does the mode stay bounded -- no unexpected leakage into
    other territory?
  - Utility proportionality: does the real estate and activation prominence
    invested in this mode match the frequency and importance of the task it
    serves? A rare task with a cheap workaround does not earn a dedicated
    prime-real-estate key.

- A note on mode overuse

  - The same framework that rescues well-designed modes also condemns
    mode-heavy designs that fail proportionality across the board. A
    mode-fanatic who adds modes for every minor task -- plausible, given how
    satisfying the namespace expansion feels -- produces a different kind of
    complexity: many small namespaces, each requiring a mental context shift,
    few of them earning their keep by the proportionality criterion.
  - The framework is a test, not a license. Modes are justified when they
    pass all five criteria; they are not justified simply because they can
    be added.

- Two examples against the criteria

  - Caps Lock: fails on three of five. No reliable visual indicator
    (visibility). Accidental activation via prime real estate with minimal
    friction (switching cost -- the problem is not that switching is hard but
    that it happens when you didn't intend it). And the task -- typing in all
    capitals -- is rare, has a cheap modern workaround (type and recase), and
    occupies a permanently dedicated key on the home row (utility
    proportionality). Task clarity and coherence are fine; all-caps is a
    legitimate and coherent category. The design failures are elsewhere.

  - Vim insert/normal: passes most criteria, with caveats. The mode
    distinction maps to a real task difference (structural navigation vs.
    direct text entry) with genuine coherence. Switching cost is low and
    deliberate. The weakness is visibility: vim's default mode indicator is
    minimal, and users do lose track. The vim advocate's response -- that the
    tax rate drops to near zero once the modes are internalized and
    because vim's undo system is so powerful/easy to use -- is true but
    doesn't excuse the weak default. A better mode indicator could
    have been built into vim from the outset.

- The jujitsu: Raskin's automaticity argument supports good modes

  - Raskin's strongest argument against modes is about automaticity: the
    state where a practiced skill executes without conscious attention. Modes
    prevent automaticity from forming reliably, or cause it to misfire -- the
    mode error, where a practiced habit fires in the wrong context.
  - This is correct as a description of *bad* modes. But look at what the
    argument actually requires: consistency and visible state. Those are
    exactly the first two criteria for a good mode.
  - A mode that satisfies the four criteria gives automaticity a stable
    surface to form on. The user learns one behavior set for navigation, a
    different one for text entry. Neither interferes with the other because
    transitions are deliberate and visible. No mode errors; no invisible
    state; no inconsistency.
  - Raskin correctly diagnosed what makes modes fail. He drew the wrong
    conclusion. The solution to bad modes is not the absence of modes -- it
    is modes designed to the criteria his own argument implies.

- Transition to S6

  - Modes, done well, solve one problem: they expand the available command
    namespace without modifiers, by making context do the work that held keys
    would otherwise do. But solving the namespace problem is only half the
    job. Within any mode, you still need a command vocabulary a user can
    actually learn and remember. That is a separate problem -- and it is
    where the modifier tradition, even at its best, has always fallen short.

## S6: Grammar and multi-key bindings

- Transition in: pick up the planted thread from S2. Modifiers solve
  real estate but destroy memorability. Within a well-designed mode, the
  same problem recurs: how do you give a user hundreds of commands they
  can actually retain? The answer is grammar.

- What grammar means here

  - Not natural language grammar -- a narrower claim: a small set of
    combinatorial rules that generates a large command vocabulary, where the
    meaning of any combination is derivable from the meaning of its parts.
  - Structure does the memorization work. You don't memorize each binding as
    an atom; you learn the components and their combinations follow.
  - Vim as the canonical example:
    - VERB: an operation (insert, append, delete, yank/copy, change)
    - NOUN/MOTION: a text object (char, word, line, paragraph, sentence) or
      a range defined by a motion (2 paragraphs, 3 words, to bottom of screen)
    - QUANTIFIER: a number prefixing the verb, noun, or both
    - `d3w` is not memorized as a unit. You know `d` (delete), `3` (count),
      `w` (word). The combination is inferrable.
    - A small vocabulary of components generates a very large command space.

- The real-estate math (development of the S2 planted point)

  - Simplified model: 35 keys (letters plus common punctuation), one
    modifier (SHIFT).
  - Modifier approach: 35 × 2 = 70 bindings. Each key participates in
    at most two commands.
  - Multi-key sequential approach: 35 × 35 = 1,225 two-key combinations.
    Three-key sequences multiply further.
  - The quantity argument matters, but the mnemonic-space argument matters
    more: in the modifier scheme, each key is locked into one or two
    arbitrary assignments. In the multi-key scheme, each key can serve as
    a prefix for a whole family of related commands -- `v` for all
    video-related operations, `c` for all chapter-related operations. The
    namespace is not just larger; it is organizable by meaning.
  - Ergonomic bonus: sequential keypresses require no simultaneous
    hand contortion. Each key is pressed and released before the next.

- Cognitive science backing

  - Depth-of-processing theory: semantically encoded material -- meaning
    attached, connected to existing knowledge -- is retained far better
    than rote symbol-to-action pairs. A binding you understand why you
    pressed sticks; one you merely memorized fades.
  - Chunking and schema: the cognitive load of a grammatical binding
    system is not proportional to the number of commands. You learn the
    rules; the combinations are derivable. The schema does the
    memorization work.
  - Natural language analogy: every fluent speaker already operates a
    grammatical system orders of magnitude more complex than any
    application's command vocabulary. The cognitive machinery is there;
    grammar-based bindings plug into it. Modifier-based bindings do not.

- Modes as the outer grammar layer

  - A mode is the outermost namespace: it determines which grammar
    applies. The full sequence is [mode] [noun] [verb].
  - Applications add another outer layer: [app] [mode] [noun] [verb].
  - Modes are what let the grammar scale to large applications without
    collision. Without modes, all commands must share a single namespace;
    the grammar can only grow so far before prefixes conflict. Modes give
    each task domain its own grammar space.

- LoopLlama as the worked example

  - LL's binding scheme is the concrete instance of everything argued above.
    This is where the essay's occasion and its argument converge.
  - Entity vocabulary, mostly mnemonic: v = video, c = chapter, s = section, l
    = loop, m = mark, j = jump, d = delete. A new user seeing `vd` can make a
    reasonable guess.
  - The most common operations get double-letter bindings, because those are
    the easiest to type. In LL, those operations are creating new entities
    (cc, ss, ll, mm), setting scratch bounds (`[[` and `]]`), and toggling
    looping (xx) to give a few examples.
  - One non-mnemonic exception: `s` was claimed by sections, so the scratch
    loop family uses `x` as its prefix. As long as exceptions are limited in
    number, the overall scheme remains learnable. The grammar carries the
    load; isolated exceptions don't break it.
  - Which-key as the discoverability mechanism: press any prefix and the
    available completions appear. Education at the moment of motivated need --
    the user is already reaching for the command when the hint appears. This
    is the scalable version of what the Cat tried to accomplish with printed
    key labels: contextual, just-in-time, not limited to a fixed domain.
  - The cognitive demands of learning LL's binding system are lower than the
    demands of learning LL's features -- which the user has to learn
    regardless. The bindings come nearly for free once the vocabulary is
    established.

- Transition to S7

  - Modes plus grammar is the answer Raskin was looking for: keyboard
    efficiency for everyone, not just experts, without custom hardware
    and without a fixed-domain appliance. The approach generalizes. The
    question the conclusion takes up is why, if this is available, the
    ecosystem didn't build that way -- and what it would take for it to.

## S7: The ecosystem problem

- Transition in: the argument is complete on the technical level. The
  remaining question is structural: why, if this approach is available
  and demonstrably superior, did the ecosystem not converge on it?

- The path of least resistance

  - For any developer building keyboard support, the default is to
    inherit OS modifier conventions. The platform supplies them; users
    recognize them; tooling expects them. Doing something else requires
    deliberate effort against the grain.
  - Developer culture, OS APIs, user expectations, and platform
    documentation all reinforce modifier-based schemes generationally.
    Each layer of the ecosystem reflects and reproduces the choices made
    by the layer above it.
  - This is not negligence or conspiracy. It is what happens when
    infrastructure is built one way and culture follows. The path of
    least resistance leads to menus and modifiers, and most developers
    take it.

- The developer who never decided

  - Most applications with keyboard support have a modifier-based shortcut
    list accessible from the preferences menu. The developers who built
    them did not make a considered choice to reject grammar-based bindings.
    The platform offered modifiers; the tooling supported modifiers; the
    documentation showed modifiers. They followed the path. The outcome
    was determined before they started.

- The stagnation (NOTE: consider whether/where to make this point)

  - The early period of personal computing was genuinely exploratory:
    Engelbart's NLS, the Canon Cat, Smalltalk, various hardware
    experiments. People were actively trying to think about better
    ways to control computers.
  - The Mac/Windows paradigm won commercially and the book closed --
    fast, and almost completely. The major OS makers have cranked out
    new versions for decades with minimal change to the fundamental
    input paradigm. The Cat, for all its flaws, was at least trying
    something different.
  - The paradigm is self-reinforcing: users have internalized it so
    thoroughly that departures feel broken rather than better. (Win8
    as an example -- wrong direction, but illustrates how hard the
    ecosystem resists change even when someone tries.)
  - The tragedy is not only that good systems are the hard path -- it
    is also that the window for rethinking may have closed. The
    infrastructure, culture, and user expectations now all point the
    same way.

- Vim and LoopLlama swim upstream

  - Individual counter-examples prove the approach works. Vim is the
    canonical case: decades of sustained use across millions of users,
    despite (or because of) a grammar-based binding system that the
    ecosystem offered no help in building.
  - LoopLlama is a smaller case: a single-developer tool that chose the
    harder path and got a more learnable, more memorable binding system
    as a result.
  - But both are exceptions maintained by people who chose to swim
    against the current. The ecosystem sets the median outcome, not the
    exceptions. Requiring exceptional deliberateness from every developer
    to produce adequate UX is not a solution; it is a description of
    the problem.

- The counterfactual

  - If the major OS makers had built culture and infrastructure oriented
    around structured, namespace-based binding systems -- grammar-based
    binding toolkits, which-key as a platform primitive, documentation
    standards for hierarchical schemes -- the path of least resistance
    would lead somewhere better.
  - Even developers who never think carefully about UX would produce
    better defaults by following the platform. The median outcome would
    shift without requiring every developer to be exceptional.
  - The tragedy is not that good systems are impossible or even difficult
    to design. It is that the ecosystem was built in a way that makes
    them the hard path -- and then reproduced that structure across
    decades of software.
