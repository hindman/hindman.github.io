## DRAFT PLAN

- Section 1: The UX choice

  - State the problem: mouse is the wrong tool for routine commands
    - Right for spatial tasks (pointing, dragging, positioning)
    - Wrong for issuing commands: slow, ergonomically costly, non-repeatable
    - Akin to a caveman pointing at the stone he wants.

  - The problem was recognized early, and serious people proposed
    solutions. Two worth examining:

    - Stephenson (one efficient paragraph):
      - His target is GUI opacity, not the mouse per se -- but he
        names the right symptom: keyboard-centric, expert-friendly
        tools are more powerful
          - efficient
          - repeatable, automatable, flexible, chainable
          - less passive, more creative culture of computing
      - His solution: embrace the CLI, join the tribe of experts
      - The limitation: only works for a small group willing to
        invest; most users won't; it's a solution for a subset, not
        a general answer
      - This is the "expert pole" -- the conventional reach among
        people who take the problem seriously

    - Raskin (set up for the rest of the essay):
      - The empiricist: he measured mouse inefficiency with real
        science (Fitts's Law, KLM)
      - Tried to solve it for everyone, not just experts
      - His solution: domain-specific hardware (Canon Cat) -- proved
        the concept but couldn't generalize
      - Point reader forward: his diagnosis is right; the essay's
        argument is what finally generalizes it

  - The essay's claim: a software design pattern -- grammar plus
    modes -- generalizes Raskin's insight without Stephenson's
    expertise requirement; it can serve both ends of the spectrum

- Section 2: The modifier trap

  - Apps with keyboard support inherit OS modifier conventions (CMD, CTRL,
    SHIFT) -- the path of least resistance set by the ecosystem
    - Developer tooling, expectations, and OS culture all reinforce
      modifier-based schemes generationally
  - Modifiers mitigate the real-estate problem: you can have more bindings
  - But they wreck memorability: abstract, no inherent meaning, prime letters
    go fast, the rest becomes a mess
  - For any large app, you need both quantity and memorability; modifiers
    deliver one and destroy the other

- Section 3: Modes

  - Modes solve the real-estate problem without modifiers: same key means
    different things in different contexts
  - The emacs objection: tracking an invisible variable is a real cognitive
    tax, especially when navigation and entry are interleaved
  - Answer: the cost is per-transition, not per-command; a mode indicator
    makes it visible; modes map to genuine task phases already happening
  - The critique hits hardest for text editors -- most apps have far lower
    transition frequency (LoopLlama as example)

- Section 4: Grammar

  - Grammars solve the memorability problem: a small set of rules generates
    many derivable combinations
  - LoopLlama as example: NOUN-VERB structure, mnemonic vocabulary (v=video,
    c=chapter...), two-letter combos
  - The structure does the memorization work -- you don't have to remember
    `vd`; you know what `v` and `d` mean
  - A mode is the first (outermost) layer of the grammar -- the full sequence
    is [mode] [noun] [verb]; modes are what let the grammar scale
  - Grammatical systems are:
    - Easier to learn:
      - All humans are already expert at something harder: natural language; a
        grammar-based binding scheme leverages the same cognitive machinery
      - Footnote: Cognitive science backs this: depth-of-processing theory
        says semantically encoded material sticks far better than rote
        symbol-to-action pairs
    - Easier to teach:
      - Which-key navigates the hierarchy at every layer: press a prefix, see
        available completions -- education at the moment of motivated need

- Section 5: Conclusion

  - Return to the S0 choice: mouse vs. Stephenson -- but a well-designed
    system is a third option
  - Even casual users absorb a well-designed system; meaning-attached
    bindings are stickier; which-key lowers the entry cost further
  - Individual counter-examples (Vim, LoopLlama) swim upstream -- the
    ecosystem sets the median, not the exceptions
  - The knitting app could have been better; the ecosystem gave its
    developers no reason and no tools to do so

## LoopLlama and the tragedy of computer UX

Mouse as an anti-pattern. A computer mouse is a great invention for certain
kinds of tasks: things that, by their nature, inherently involve actual
pointing, positioning, dragging. But they are an absurd way to issue routine
commands to a machine -- akin to a caveman pointing at the stone he wants.
Basic navigation, frequent operations -- none of it is a good fit for a tool
that requires you to grab, position, click. That kind of UX is not efficient
as it relates to time or ergonomics. It is also less repeatable, scriptable,
automatable, and scalable. The keyboard works better for that. Fast, literally
alread at hand.

Keyboard modifiers as an anti-pattern. Even applications with strong key
binding support tend to go astray because they take for granted the
conventions inherited from the largest operating systems. Key binding schemes
tend to quickly because awkward and difficult to remember beyond the basics.
Do I need CMD, CTRL, or SHIFT for that one? This may seem like a mere rehash
of classic vim vs emacs debates on the merits of chordal key bindings --
admittedly, there is some of that. But my intended point goes deeper. For key
bindings to work in medium-to-large applications two things are required: (1)
we need a lot of key binding real estate to find homes for all of the
operastions, and (2) the bindings need to be memorable. The typical binding
approach solves #1 by resorting to various modifier combos. But that causes #2
to crash. Modifiers are abstract entities, with no inherent meaning. Some of
the most important operations will get a good binding: eg, CMD-C for copy and
CMD-P for print are easy enough to remember. But in this system, the prime
real estate (the best letters) go fast. The end result almost always tends to
be a mess.

Modes as a pattern. On the surface, this point is more vim-vs-emacs. But,
again, I want to go deeper. Vim ends up with easier to type and remember key
bindings than emacs because it decides to pay a small cost up front: modes.
It's a simple idea: in some contexts, pressing `x` might do some operation; in
a different context, `x` is still open real estate for your binding needs.
Yes, the user must get used to switching modes -- but those switches are also
easy to execute in a system where the main operations require nothing more
than single key presses. Many classic apps are even better candidates for
modal key bindings than text editors. Consider Excel. For a mainstream app its
bindings are OK: they can be memorized to increase your speed and efficiency.
But doing so takes some effort and some of the moves are awkward. Now
imagine Excel with modes. All sorts of navigational and cell-selection
maneuvers can now be done with mnemonic key presses, without any modifiers.
Yes, in those moments when you are actually performing data entry, you need to
switch modes. But as a former heavy Excel user I know that the majority of
time is spent on tasks other than direct data entry.

Grammars as a pattern. How do you create a powerful, scalable, and mnemonic
binding scheme? By embracing a grammar. Vim does it: there are operations
(insert, append, delete, copy) and their are entities (text objects and spans
of text defined by motions). In other words, VERB then NOUN. LL v2 makes a
similar move. Its binding scheme -- and the often parallel menu scheme --
follow a NOUN then VERB approach. Under this strategy you need a letter for
each noun or entity in your system and you need a letter for the various
operations. Even though there are very few letters, you can cover a lot of
ground using 2-letter combos. In LL, nearly all of the letters have a strong
mmenonic tie (v=video, c=chapter, j=jump, d=delete). The most common or
meaningful operations get the double-letter bindings: cc, ss, ll, mm to create
chapters, sections, saved loops, marks. In some cases, non-mnemonic choices
were required: `s` was claimed by sections, so the scratch loop bindings had
to use `x` for their prefix. As long as the number of such resorts is limited,
a typical person can memorize the scheme.

Memory and compositional grammar. What a memory researcher would notice about
Vim is compositional grammar. You don't memorize d3w as an atom; you learn d
(delete), 3 (count), w (word) and the combination is inferrable. Cognitive
psychology calls this chunking plus schema: a small set of learned rules
generates a large number of derivable combinations. The schema does the
memorization work for you. Emacs has no equivalent — each command is its own
thing to learn.

Multi-key bindings more powerful than chords. The modifier-free approach to
bindings works better than the chordal approach for several reasons. They
scale better: 26 letters x 26 letters = 676 bindings. Versus: 26 letters * 4
modifiers = 104. Yes, you can create more modifier combos, but at even greater
mental cost. Multi-key schemes can be connected directly to grammatical or
other thematic schemes, helping the binding system to be more memorable. The
bindings become a kind of language, with structure. Humans excel at learning
material like this. Modifier-based binding schemes tend to lack that kind of
structure: they quickly become long lists of things to memorize (computers are
good at that, not humans) rather than meaningful entities operating within
some kind of logical structure.

Not the Stephenson argument. The classic pro-keyboard arguments -- Stephenson,
Engelbart, the Unix fans -- mostly argue that keyboard-centric tools give more
power to expert users. True, but that is a different argument. My claim: a
well-designed modal, structured, mnemonic binding scheme can supply better UX
for both expert and casual users alike. The case does not rest on users
becoming power users. And I'm a realist: very few users will ever be power
users in any given tool.

Engelbart on the mouse. Engelbart invented the mouse as half of a two-part
system. The other half was a chorded keyboard -- a 5-key left-hand device
where simultaneous key combinations (like piano chords) produced characters
and commands. Right hand for pointing, left hand for commanding. The mouse was
adopted; the keyset was not. His insight about the mouse is exactly right: it
is the right tool for spatial and positional tasks -- selecting regions,
navigating 2D space, pointing at structure. His frustration was that the
keyset was abandoned, leaving users with only the pointing half of a system
designed as two-handed.

The language argument. Most people will never be Vim masters. Stephenson-style
arguments expect too much of humanity. But all humans are already expert at
something much harder than Vim: natural language. Language has grammar
(structure), vocabulary (entities and operations), and meaning baked together.
Humans absorb this not because they are especially smart but because the
system has coherent, meaningful structure that maps onto real things. A
binding scheme built around STRUCTURE / GRAMMAR / MEANING / MNEMONIC leverages
the same cognitive machinery that makes language learnable. The cognitive
demands of a well-designed binding system can be lower than the demands of
learning the app's features themselves -- which you have to learn regardless.

Cognitive stickiness. The "meaning-attached bindings are stickier" observation
has real cognitive science behind it. Depth-of-processing theory (Craik &
Lockhart, 1972): information encoded with semantic content is retained far
better than rote symbol-to-action pairs. Mnemonics work because they attach
arbitrary signals to meaning already in long-term memory. Modifier-based
schemes force rote learning. A structured, mnemonic scheme allows semantic
encoding. This is not just intuition about UX; it is the lab finding applied
to binding design. Observable at the personal level over decades of learning
apps: memorable bindings stick; bindings with no inherent logic can stick if
used constantly (you pay the tax); but meaning-attached bindings are
inherently and durably stickier.

Which-key as the bridge to casual users. Press a prefix, see the available
completions. Not documentation -- in-context revelation at the moment of
motivated need. That is exactly when learning sticks. A casual user who
presses `s` and sees a structured menu of section operations is being educated
without being asked to study. The system starts easy and progressively
educates any curious user as they use it, in meaningful bits, without opening
any documentation. This is the key mechanism for making a structured binding
scheme accessible below the expert end of the spectrum.

Qualifications and realism. Many users have limited needs. Many needs are
inherently limited. The LL help doc ends its quick start by saying "most of
you can stop reading." A casual user will click along happily. But even a
casual user will pick up core bindings if they use the tool frequently enough.
Everyone is a casual user somewhere. A long-time Vim devotee may barely touch
Powerpoint. A guitarist who uses LoopLlama daily may show no interest in
mastering Excel. The argument is not that everyone will internalize a full
binding system. It is that even occasional users will absorb some of a
well-designed system -- especially if which-key is there to help -- and that
even casual users have their domains. A heavy knitting-app user would benefit
from a thoughtful binding scheme if that culture had developed differently.

The ecosystem argument. The most structural point in the whole critique. The
failure of good binding design in mainstream apps is not simply that
developers made bad choices. The OS and the surrounding culture shaped what
was possible and expected. In the current ecosystem -- flat, modifier-based,
culturally entrenched -- even developers who care about keyboard ergonomics
produce worse binding schemes than they otherwise would. The path of least
resistance leads to menus and modifiers. If the major OS makers had developed
a culture and infrastructure oriented around structured, namespace-based
binding systems, even less conscientious developers would produce better
schemes by default. Individual counter-examples -- Vim, Emacs, LoopLlama --
exist within a bad ecosystem and are swimming upstream. They do not move the
median. The median is set by the platform.

Two mechanisms for the local maximum. Why are we stuck? Not just user habit,
though that matters. The deeper lock-in is developer tooling, developer
expectations, and OS-level cultural norms -- all compounding over time. Each
generation of developers learns to build flat modifier-based schemes because
that is what the platform rewards and what users expect. The knitting app is
not built well not because its developers are lazy, but because the ecosystem
gives them no reason and no tools to do better.

Vim versus emacs: the real steelman. The classic vim arguments are well-known.
The emacs case deserves a genuine hearing. Its strongest argument is
cognitive, not ergonomic: modal editing requires tracking an invisible
variable at all times -- what mode am I in? -- which is a real tax when
editing is interleaved with reading, thinking, and context-switching. The
emacs model says there is only one mode; Ctrl and Meta are the admission
price. A second argument: emacs key bindings are the readline defaults,
meaning bash, zsh, the Python REPL, and most terminal programs all use them.
Learn emacs bindings and you get them for free across the terminal ecosystem.
A third: M-x gives every function a name, making the editor self-documenting
-- you can find a feature without knowing its binding.

Reassessing the emacs case. On closer inspection, several of those arguments
are weaker than they appear. The readline ubiquity claim is an argument about
network effects, not design quality -- and it gets the causal arrow backwards.
Emacs key bindings predate readline; GNU readline was explicitly designed to
replicate emacs-style line editing in bash. "Emacs bindings are everywhere" is
a consequence of emacs influence, not an independent reason to prefer the
model. The Lisp extensibility argument and the discoverability argument are
real advantages -- but both are orthogonal to key binding design. Vim could
have had a better scripting environment (and now has one, via Lua). Better
discoverability infrastructure could have been built on top of vim long ago,
and increasingly has been. These are tooling gaps, not structural features of
the binding model.

Emacs' partial grammar. Emacs bindings do have some structure. The clearest
pattern is Meta-as-amplifier: Ctrl-f moves one character, Meta-f moves one
word; Ctrl-d deletes a character, Meta-d deletes a word; Ctrl-k kills to end
of line, Meta-k kills to end of sentence. That is a genuine two-level grammar
where the modifier encodes scope. Prefix namespaces -- Ctrl-x for file and
buffer operations, Ctrl-c for mode-local bindings -- impose further
organization. But the structure is shallower than vim's generative grammar:
more lookup table than language. And the prefix namespaces are themselves an
implicit concession that flat chordal bindings do not scale.

Emacs halfway to wisdom. The Ctrl-x completion display -- press the prefix,
see all available continuations -- is essentially the which-key idea. When
emacs advocates cite this as a discoverability strength, they are endorsing
exactly the principle that makes structured prefix namespaces good:
enumerable, browsable completions revealed at the moment of need. The insight
points directly toward the vim leader-key model, where the main command
namespace requires no modifier at all. But emacs cannot escape its foundation.
Ctrl is load-bearing all the way down. The right idea was found; the original
sin was already baked in too deep to undo.

Vim's grammar and the which-key objection. One common claim: vim's generative
verb-motion grammar is structurally resistant to which-key style help. After
`d`, the valid completions are "any motion" -- a semantic category, not a
finite list. But this argument is overstated. The actual usage distribution is
heavily concentrated. A curated display of the 25 or 30 most common motions
and text objects in operator-pending mode would cover the vast majority of
real use. Exhaustiveness is not the standard; useful coverage is. An
additional keypress could surface a paginated reference -- exactly how
well-designed CLI help works. The gap is tooling culture, not structural
impossibility. Vim's culture skewed toward RTFM and muscle memory for long
enough that the infrastructure was never built. It can be.

Vim-style spreadsheets. Spreadsheets are arguably a better fit for modal
editing than text editors. The majority of time in a spreadsheet is not data
entry -- it is navigation, selection, formatting, review, formula inspection.
That is exactly the work that modal single-key commands handle well: insert
mode for cell entry, everything else driven by mnemonic key presses, no
modifiers required. This was noticed. sc (Spreadsheet Calculator), a Unix
terminal program from the early 1980s, had hjkl navigation from the start. Its
modern fork, sc-im, has genuine modal editing: normal mode for navigation and
commands, insert mode for cell entry, a vim-style command line. VisiData is a
related terminal tool for tabular data exploration with vim-inspired
navigation -- not a formula-driven spreadsheet, but adjacent in spirit. The
mainstream never picked this up. Classic Excel's own model was implicitly
modal -- navigation state versus cell-edit state, the latter triggered by F2
or typing -- but the abstraction was never made explicit and never exploited
for keybinding design. The extensive keyboard support in classic Windows Excel
was genuinely powerful, arriving through a patchwork of special keys rather
than a coherent modal scheme. A near miss.

## PLANNING NOTES: Raskin / Canon Cat as framing

### Stephenson: drop or minimize

Stephenson's target in "In the Beginning" is the GUI as metaphor -- the
desktop, icons, hidden complexity. His "tribe of experts" values
transparency and direct machine access, not specifically keyboard
efficiency. His argument is philosophical and cultural, not ergonomic.
Using him as a stand-in for "people who prefer keyboard over mouse" is
a misfit. The essay's real argument is about control interface design,
not about GUI opacity. Replace Stephenson with Raskin as the primary
historical witness.

### Raskin and the Canon Cat: ally on diagnosis, target on solutions

Raskin correctly identifies the core problems the essay addresses:

- Mouse inefficiency for routine commands -- backed by real science
  (Fitts's Law, Keystroke-Level Model). This is the essay's strongest
  empirical anchor and Raskin is the right citation for it.
- GUI menus becoming as opaque as old CLIs -- Canon's own 1987
  marketing made this point explicitly.
- The need for expert-friendly control mechanisms that also serve
  casual users.

The Canon Cat is a vivid historical artifact: LEAP keys (two keys
below the spacebar, operated by thumbs) implement a quasimode --
held-key state, vim-like single mnemonic letters while held. The
physical key labels are the hardware equivalent of which-key:
discoverability baked into the device. An interesting emacs/vim hybrid
in its structure.

But Raskin's solutions don't scale:

- The Cat is a single-domain appliance. The key-label approach works
  because the domain is fixed and finite.
- Economic logic guarantees general-purpose computing wins. People
  want one device that does many things, not a long list of appliances.
- The appliance approach hides complexity by removing it upstream at
  design time. That doesn't transfer.

Use Raskin/Cat as: someone who saw the problem clearly and built the
most rigorous critique, proved the concept with working hardware, but
within a scope that couldn't generalize. The essay's claim is that
modes plus grammar accomplish in software what the Cat accomplished in
hardware, without the domain ceiling.

### The modes reframing -- most important planning note

Raskin's anti-modes position is incoherent as an absolute claim.
Applications are modes: pressing `x` in Excel and in LoopLlama produce
different results. A ZUI would smuggle in mode-like differences not
labeled as such. In general-purpose computing, context-mechanisms that
expand the available command space are not optional -- they are
inevitable. The question was never whether modes exist; it was always
what properties make them cognitively manageable.

Raskin actually discovered the right answer to that question, even
though he misidentified the remedy. What he found is a set of
properties that distinguish good from bad context-mechanisms:

- Is the current state visible to the user?
- Does the state map to a task distinction the user already recognizes?
- Is the transition cost low and deliberate?
- Does the context have coherent, bounded meaning?

Caps Lock fails all four. Vi's insert/normal distinction passes all four -- it
maps to a real task boundary, the transition is one keystroke, the indicator
is available [the visual indicator in default vim is pretty weak: maybe 3 out
of 4; vim could have done a lot better to mitigate that problem].
Applications-as-modes are fine because the context switch is deliberate and
visible.

The essay's position: Raskin asked the wrong question. The right
question is what properties a context-mechanism must have to be
cognitively manageable. That is the question the essay answers, and
Raskin never did. Cite him for the diagnosis; use his failure to
generalize as the setup for the essay's own prescription.

## PLANNING NOTES: Raskin's diagnosis of the status quo; automaticity

### Raskin as "registered independent"

Cat (1987) and The Humane Interface (2000) both appeared when Raskin
knew the state of the art: early PCs, Mac, Windows, and the Unix/CLI
culture Stephenson celebrates. His position was neither pro-GUI-as-
implemented nor pro-CLI. He found both wanting. Stephenson is a CLI
partisan; Raskin is a registered independent.

### His critique of GUIs

- Modes everywhere: dialogs capturing focus, application states,
  Caps Lock, confirmation prompts with context-dependent consequences.
  GUIs as implemented were riddled with the thing he considered most
  dangerous.
- Desktop metaphor is historically contingent, not cognitively
  natural. Files and folders map onto mainframe filing conventions,
  not cognitive research. Worth noting: the user's pushback on this
  is valid -- hierarchical organization is genuinely natural; humans
  use it spontaneously. Raskin's stronger and more defensible claim
  is narrower: the pure hierarchy doesn't accommodate all natural
  organizational strategies (a document belonging to two projects),
  and many implementation details reflect engineering history rather
  than cognitive fit.
- Menus growing as opaque as old CLIs: Canon's 1987 marketing made
  this point explicitly. Raskin was not straightforwardly anti-menu
  -- his alternatives (LEAP, Archy autocomplete) were discoverability
  mechanisms too. His objection was menus as the primary navigation
  mechanism, requiring users to know where in the hierarchy to look.
  A menu that supplements a binding scheme, or a which-key display
  that reveals hierarchy on demand, is not what he was attacking.
  LL's menus and key-binding listings are legitimate educational
  tools by his own logic.
- Mouse as primary control: the efficiency critique covered earlier.
- Inconsistency across applications: breaks automaticity (see below).

### His critique of CLI

CLI was not his primary target -- by 2000 it had already lost the
mainstream. His general objections: memorability burden (recall rather
than recognition), exact syntax with no tolerance for approximation,
no visual feedback on system state, steep learning curve. He wanted
something different from both poles, not a return to the CLI.

### Automaticity -- relevant to the modes argument

Automaticity is the state where a practiced skill executes without
conscious attention, freeing cognitive resources for higher-level
work. A new driver thinks "mirror, signal, shoulder check"; an
experienced driver does it without thinking. The goal for frequent
interface operations is the same: get them to automaticity so the
user's mind is free to focus on the task, not the tool.

Raskin's point: several interface properties actively prevent
automaticity from forming, or cause it to misfire:

- Inconsistency: if the same action sometimes does different things,
  you can never safely let the behavior become automatic.
- Mode errors are the worst case: the user has practiced "press x,
  character deleted" to automaticity -- then fires it in the wrong
  mode. The practiced skill becomes a liability.
- Interface changes (moved menus, changed shortcuts across versions)
  destroy automaticity already built, imposing re-learning costs
  users feel viscerally even when they can't name the cause.

### The jujitsu move on automaticity

Raskin's automaticity argument is actually an argument for
well-designed modes, not against modes per se. His real concern is
invisible state and inconsistency -- those prevent automaticity from
forming and cause it to misfire.

A mode that maps to a genuine task boundary, has a visible indicator,
and maintains consistent behavior within that mode does none of those
things. It gives automaticity a stable surface to form on. The user
learns one behavior set for navigation mode, another for entry mode;
neither interferes with the other because transitions are deliberate
and visible.

The misfire problem -- his strongest example -- only occurs when the
mode is invisible. A mode indicator solves it directly. The argument
chain for the essay: Raskin correctly identifies that automaticity
requires consistency and visible state; he concludes modes are bad;
but the actual requirement is that state must be visible and behavior
consistent within each state. Modes satisfying those conditions
support automaticity -- arguably better than a modeless system that
must pack all behaviors into one namespace and resort to less
memorable mechanisms (modifiers) to differentiate them.

## PLANNING NOTES: Scoring modes against the four criteria

Recap of the four criteria for a well-designed context-mechanism:

1. Is the current state visible to the user?
2. Does the state map to a task distinction the user already recognizes?
3. Is the transition cost low and deliberate?
4. Does the context have coherent, bounded meaning?

Caps Lock fails all four: no reliable indicator, no task distinction
it maps to, transitions are accidental rather than deliberate, and the
mode has no coherent bounded meaning -- it just silently inverts case.

Default vim passes 2, 3, and 4 cleanly:
- The normal/insert distinction maps to a real task boundary the user
  already recognizes (navigating/commanding vs. entering text).
- Transitions are cheap: one keystroke in either direction.
- Each mode has coherent, bounded meaning; the namespaces don't bleed.

But vim partially fails criterion 1. The default visual indicator
(the mode display in the statusline) is subtle enough that beginners
routinely miss it, and even experienced users occasionally fire a
command in the wrong mode. This is a well-known vim tax. The vim
advocate's response is that the tax rate is low once the modes are
internalized -- which is true but doesn't excuse the weak default.

The important point: this is a tooling and culture failure, not a
structural one. The mode design is correct in principle; the indicator
was an afterthought. Vim with a well-configured statusline or
colorscheme (distinct cursor shape per mode, prominent indicator)
passes all four. The gap could have been closed in 1991 if the culture
had prioritized discoverability.

This connects to the ecosystem argument: even a well-designed modal
system shipped with weak feedback infrastructure because the culture
didn't demand it. Which-key and better statuslines are exactly the
remedies for criterion 1 failures -- software solutions to what was
always a solvable problem.

## PLANNING NOTES: Canon Cat in detail -- findings from the manual

### LEAP in practice

LEAP is literal text search, incremental (cursor moves as you type),
quasimodal (held thumb key, no Enter required). Closest precedent is
emacs C-s. The no-Enter advantage is real for short queries. But for
longer queries requiring correction, the thumb must stay pinned to
the LEAP key throughout -- awkward one-handed typing. The quasimode
eliminates mode confusion but introduces a physical constraint the
theory glosses over. For short jumps: genuine improvement over vim
`/`. For longer queries: the tradeoff is less favorable.

### Text objects -- indirect but present

The Cat has structural navigation, but expressed through LEAP rather
than dedicated primitives:
- Word: LEAP to a space
- Sentence: LEAP to a period
- Paragraph: LEAP to two returns
- Page: LEAP via a dedicated PAGE button

This works but requires a mental translation -- "move to next word"
expressed as "find a space." Indirect in a way that vim's `w` is not.
The structural unit is not the target; its delimiter is.

### Creeping

Users found a way to use LEAP for character-by-character positional
navigation (called "creeping"). The manual explicitly admonishes
against it. Unlike vim's culture of nudging away from hjkl while
leaving the key functional, the Cat's design made creeping physically
awkward -- the hardware itself discouraged what users needed. The
warning reflects a real gap, not just a style preference.

### Marks -- the most damning gap

No native mark system. The manual's advice: type something distinctive
(`qq`, `**`) in the document and LEAP to it later. Users must remember
to delete these before printing. A fundamental editing primitive --
"save this location" -- handled by polluting the document. For a
system designed to revolutionize navigation, this is a significant
failure of follow-through.

### Scrolling

Reportedly complex and poorly explained in the manual. Likely because
LEAP-only navigation has no natural answer to "move viewport without
specifying a target" -- scrolling had to be bolted on separately and
didn't integrate cleanly.

### Raskin shipped a mode

Wide vs. narrow cursor affects erase direction (and possibly other
operations). By Raskin's own definition -- same key, different
behavior depending on state -- this is a mode. The cursor shape makes
the state somewhat visible (arguably better than default vim), but the
surface area of mode-dependent behavior may extend beyond erase. This
is the clearest evidence that modes are inevitable; even their most
ideological opponent shipped one.

### The thumb cluster

Two LEAP keys below the spacebar, operated by thumbs: a genuine
ergonomic insight. Thumbs are the strongest fingers and spend most of
their time on a single key; pinkies handle Shift/Ctrl despite being
the weakest. The load distribution on standard keyboards is backwards.
The Cat got this right in 1987. Modern ergonomic keyboards (Kinesis,
Moonlander) rediscovered it decades later, but only in the niche
market. Another good idea the ecosystem failed to absorb.

### Overall verdict

The LEAP insight is real; the quasimode implementation is elegant; the
thumb placement is smart. But the system forces everything through one
mechanism. Where LEAP fits naturally the result is clean; where it
doesn't (marks, scrolling, structural navigation), the patches are
conspicuous. A design philosophy problem, not just execution: Raskin's
ideological commitment to LEAP left legitimate user needs
underserved -- and in the marks case, handled with a workaround that
predates word processors. Proof of concept for search-driven
navigation; not a fully realized editing system.
