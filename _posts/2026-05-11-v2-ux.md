All software faces a basic question: how does the user tell it what to do?
Since the 1980s the dominant model has been drearily consistent: reach for the
mouse, navigate into an application's menus, and click the needed operation.
Ordinary navigational tasks are similar, minus the menus: reach, point,
scroll, click. Keyboard shortcuts for some of those operations do exist.
Nearly all users learn a handful — copy, paste, print, arrow keys — and stop
there. Some users invest real effort to learn the bindings of applications
they use heavily, but even the office expert in Excel eventually hits a
ceiling: the brain will hold only so many arbitrary CTRL-ALT combinations.

I recently completed [LoopLlama][llv2], a browser-based tool for close study
of YouTube videos. The app has menus and familiar mouse-oriented controls such
as buttons, dropdowns, and toggles. But at its core, LoopLlama has a
keyboard-first design: my priority was to be able to do everything efficiently
with simple key presses while holding a guitar and wearing a thumb pick,
without tedious pointing and clicking. Building it sharpened my convictions
about keyboard design and produced a working example of a better UX paradigm.

## S1: The mouse problem

The mouse is a spatial instrument designed for actions that are inherently
about location: pointing, dragging, positioning, drawing — tasks with a direct
mapping between hand movement and cursor position.

Command input is not a spatial task. When a user reaches for the mouse to
invoke a menu operation or perform a routine navigation, nothing about that
operation requires pointing. The action is discrete: the user wants to save a
file, undo a change, or toggle a setting. The mouse arrives at the target by
the same mechanism it uses to draw a curve — slow physical travel, target
acquisition, and click — but the curve-drawing precision is wasted. You don't
need a pointing instrument to say "save." Like a caveman, you point for
routine operations only when you lack the words.

The costs are individually tiny but undeniably real in aggregate. Across a day
of routine work, the many mouse traversals compounds into a significant
ergonomic load. Mouse operations are also not readily scripted, chained, or
automated. The keyboard, by contrast, is already where the hands are. Its
input is discrete and precise, exactly what command input requires. The case
for it as the natural command instrument is not subtle.

The problem was recognized early, by serious people with empirical tools to
measure it.

Douglas Engelbart, who invented the mouse, understood this division of
labor. His system paired the mouse with the keyboard: the mouse for
spatial tasks, the keyboard for text entry and commands.[^1] Only the
pointing half of that bargain survived contact with the marketplace.
The resulting arrangement — mouse for everything, including commands —
was not what he had in mind.

Jef Raskin made the case in rigorous terms in "The Humane Interface" (2000),
drawing on two tools from the empirical literature. [Fitts's Law][fitts_law]
provides a predictive model for target-acquisition time as a function of
distance and target size; applied to command input, it quantifies the cost of
keyboard-to-mouse traversal on every mouse-issued command. The
[Keystroke-Level Model][kslm], a framework for predicting task completion time
from its physical components, confirms the pattern: mouse operations are
consistently and measurably slower than keyboard equivalents for discrete
commands.

## S2: The keyboard tradition and its limits

- Transition in: the keyboard-centric tradition did exist and did develop the
  answer -- just not for everyone.

- The tradition

  - Unix, CLI, power-user culture: a whole world built around keyboard
    primacy. Commands are text; text is fast; the expert's tools are
    genuinely more powerful -- repeatable, automatable, scriptable, chainable.

- Modifiers: the structural backbone

  - The keyboard has a real-estate problem. A standard keyboard has roughly
    26 letters plus punctuation. An application with hundreds of commands
    needs hundreds of bindings. The letters run out fast.
  - The ecosystem's engineering answer is modifier keys: CTRL, CMD, ALT,
    SHIFT. Hold a modifier while pressing a letter, and you multiply the
    keyspace. CTRL alone doubles it; CTRL+SHIFT doubles it again. The
    tradition built its entire command vocabulary on this foundation.
  - Modifiers are also the part of the keyboard tradition that reached
    mainstream GUI software: CMD+C, CTRL+Z, and their kin. The shallow end
    of the CLI world's deep well.

- The modifier's inherent weaknesses

  - Multiplying the keyspace solves real estate but creates a new problem:
    memorability.
  - Modifier bindings are arbitrary symbol-to-action pairs. CMD+C for copy,
    CMD+X for cut -- why X? The mnemonic logic runs out almost immediately.
    Prime letters get claimed by the most common operations; everything else
    is assigned by convention or whim.
  - The result in any large application: a flat list of two-key combinations
    with no inherent structure/meaning, no narrative, no grammar [perhaps
    avoid mentioning grammar here: too early; not yet established]. Expert
    users absorb the most common ones through repetition. The rest remain
    perpetually uncertain -- looked up, forgotten, looked up again.
  - There is also an ergonomic cost: modifier shortcuts require pressing two
    keys simultaneously. Held-key combinations put sustained strain on the
    hand, especially for high-frequency operations.
  - The CLI tradition mostly sidesteps memorability via command *names* (which
    carry meaning) and years of accumulated muscle memory. But for the broader
    population of GUI users, modifier shortcuts are the keyboard tradition --
    and they deliver real estate at the cost of learnability.

- Stephenson as the articulate advocate

  - "In the Beginning Was the Command Line" (1999) is the most widely read
    case for this tradition.
  - His primary target is not the mouse per se but GUI opacity: the desktop
    metaphor, the hidden complexity, the passive consumer relationship with
    software that GUIs encourage.
  - His argument is philosophical and cultural, not ergonomic: the "tribe of
    experts" who value transparency and direct machine access produce a
    different -- and better -- relationship with computing.
  - Keyboard efficiency is a byproduct of that relationship, not the primary
    claim. But it is real: the expert's tools are faster, more flexible, more
    powerful.

- The expertise barrier

  - Stephenson's answer is: join the tribe. Invest in expertise. The returns
    are worth it.
  - That is true for people willing and able to make the investment. It is not
    a general answer.
  - Most people will never be expert in most of the apps they use -- the app
    isn't important enough to them to justify it. The investment has to be
    earned back, and for many apps it never is.
  - The counterpoint worth acknowledging: nearly everyone has at least one app
    they use enough to warrant real expertise -- even someone who would never
    describe themselves as a power user. A knitting app, a recipe manager, a
    habit tracker. The casual-user framing undersells how motivated people can
    be in the right context. But the expertise model still leaves the majority
    of software interactions unaddressed.

- Transition to S3

  - The keyboard tradition produced a real answer -- but one locked behind an
    expertise barrier, and built on a modifier foundation that trades real
    estate for memorability. Stephenson's prescription is to embrace the
    expertise requirement. But that is a cultural choice, not a logical
    necessity. It leaves unanswered whether a keyboard-first system could be
    designed to work for people who aren't willing to invest years in it. One
    thinker looked at both the GUI world and the CLI world, found both wanting,
    and tried to answer that question empirically.

## S3: Raskin -- right problem, wrong answer

- Transition in: not a partisan of either pole; a designer and cognitive
  scientist who measured both and found both wanting.
  - Was he a cognitive scientist? My skim of Wikipedia suggests that his
    degree was in computer science.

- Who Raskin was

  - Not a participant in the GUI-vs-CLI debate -- an independent critic of
    both sides.
    - Played a leadership role in early Macintosh project at Apple.
    - But he did not lead its full development and the end result -- notably
      its heavy reliance on the mouse-and-menus UX, but it might be premature
      to state that during the bio intro -- was very much not Raskin's vision.
  - Came from design and cognitive science. His book "The Humane Interface"
    (2000) is the key text.
  - Goal: solve the efficiency problem for everyone, not just experts. An
    empirical project, not a cultural one.

- His diagnosis

  - Mouse inefficiency: already established (S1); Raskin supplied the
    science. His contribution was to insist the problem was measurable, not
    just felt.
  - GUI menus growing as opaque as the CLIs they replaced. Deep hierarchies
    require users to know where in the structure to look -- the same
    memorability burden in a different form. (Canon's own 1987 marketing made
    this point explicitly.)
  - CLI: not his primary target -- it had already lost the mainstream by
    2000. His objections: recall-heavy, exact syntax, no tolerance for
    approximation, no visual feedback on system state. He wanted something
    different from both poles.
  - Inconsistency across applications as a distinct failure: it breaks
    automaticity -- the state where a practiced skill executes without
    conscious attention. If the same action sometimes does different things,
    you can never safely let the behavior become automatic.

- His anti-modes position

  - Modes are the villain in Raskin's account.
  - A mode is a context in which the same key produces different results. The
    user must track an invisible variable at all times: what mode am I in?
  - The danger is the mode error: a practiced habit fires in the wrong
    context. "Press x, character deleted" -- learned to automaticity -- then
    executes in a mode where x does something else entirely. The practiced
    skill becomes a liability.
  - Present this sympathetically. The concern is real and the failure mode is
    concrete. Readers who have been bitten by Caps Lock or by vim's
    insert/normal switch will recognize it.

- His solution: the Canon Cat (1987)

  - A dedicated word-processing appliance, released by Canon.
  - Command input: LEAP keys -- two keys positioned below the spacebar,
    operated by the thumbs, held to activate. Primary function: incremental
    literal text search; the cursor moves as you type the query. Additional
    operations available via a second held key (Use Front): jumps and commands
    printed on the front face of various keys, making the available operations
    physically visible to the user.
  - No persistent modes was the intention.
  - The one genuine hardware contribution: placing modifier-style keys below
    the spacebar to take advantage of the thumbs. That is an ergonomic insight
    with real merit, regardless of how the surrounding system performed.
  - Labels on keyboard.

- Commercial outcome

  - The Cat sold poorly and was discontinued after roughly a year.
  - No further development; the ideas stayed in the appliance and in Raskin's
    subsequent writing.

- Transition to S4

  - The commercial failure could be attributed to market timing, pricing, or
    the fixed-domain appliance model competing against general-purpose
    computers. But the problems go deeper than the market outcome. Examined
    closely, the Cat's design contradicts several of Raskin's own principles
    -- and the solutions it did offer turn out to be variants of the problems
    it claimed to solve.

## S4: The Cat in practice

- Transition in: the commercial failure is the surface story; the design
  problems are the more instructive one.

- The scalability problem

  - The Cat is a single-domain appliance: word processing only. The
    key-label approach works because the domain is fixed and finite -- you
    can print every command on the keyboard face when there are only so many
    commands.
  - Economic logic guarantees general-purpose computing wins. People want one
    device that does many things, not a collection of appliances. The
    appliance approach hides complexity by removing it at design time; that
    strategy cannot survive contact with general-purpose software, where the
    domain is open-ended.
  - This is not a criticism of Raskin's execution -- it is a structural
    constraint on the appliance model. No amount of refinement fixes it.

- LEAP is modifiers with new hardware

  - Raskin distinguished between modes (persistent -- you can forget you're
    in them) and quasimodes (held -- active only while the key is held,
    self-terminating on release). LEAP implements his preferred quasimodal
    approach; he would have considered this a feature.
  - But quasimodes are modifiers. LEAP is a held key that changes the
    behavior of other keys. Use Front is a second held key layered on top.
    The structure is identical to CTRL and CMD; the hardware placement is
    different.
  - The thumb-key placement is the genuine ergonomic insight. Keys below the
    spacebar are more naturally positioned than perimeter modifier keys. That
    is worth acknowledging. But the underlying structure -- hold a key,
    change the meaning of other keys -- is the same modifier logic the
    keyboard tradition had always used.
  - The printed key faces (commands labeled on the front of each key) are the
    Cat's discoverability solution -- and they work only because the domain is
    fixed and finite. It is the hardware equivalent of a vim cheatsheet taped
    to the cubicle wall: useful for a beginner in a bounded system, but not a
    scalable design. Vim users grow out of the cheatsheet. Cat users never
    could, because generalizing the domain would require a different keyboard
    entirely.
  - In some respects the Cat's modifier use is more demanding than
    conventional shortcuts. A LEAP search query requires holding the key for
    the full duration of typing -- a sustained hold rather than a brief chord.
    A CLI user, who issues commands with a name and an Enter, would find the
    ergonomic burden of LEAP familiar in structure but higher in sustained
    cost.

- Smuggled modes: the wide/narrow cursor

  - The Cat's cursor had two states: wide and narrow. The state controlled
    both the direction of text erasure and whether typing would insert or
    replace existing text.
  - This is a mode by any reasonable definition. Different keys, different
    behavior, depending on context.
  - More striking: the cursor shape is exactly the mechanism vim uses to
    signal its own normal/insert and normal/replace distinctions -- the very
    thing Raskin condemned. Raskin recreated a vim-style mode, dedicated to a
    narrow domain (deletion behavior), without acknowledging it as such.

- Smuggled modes: the Forth interpreter

  - The Cat included a hidden Forth programming environment, accessible by:
    typing the literal text "Enable Forth Language," selecting it with both
    LEAP keys, holding Use Front, and pressing Answer.
  - A mode -- with ceremony-level transition cost. The ideological opposition
    to modes produced not the absence of modes but a mode that was unusually
    hard to enter and exit.

- Navigation: bad for beginners and experts alike

  - (a) Beginners blocked from the start: the Cat had no arrow keys.
    Navigation was via LEAP (text search) or "creeping" -- using the
    LEAP keys as arrows. The manual explicitly admonished users not to
    rely on creeping as a crutch. A beginner with no search query in
    mind had no obvious way to move the cursor. The system that was
    supposed to serve everyone more than CLI blocked new users at the
    gate.

  - (b) Intermediates and experts hit a low ceiling: LEAP is literal
    text search only -- no regex, no patterns. Text objects are absent;
    the manual's own workarounds (type a space to reach a word boundary,
    a period for a sentence, two returns for a paragraph) are a tell.
    MS Word has text objects. Vim's motion vocabulary is vast. The Cat's
    navigation model tops out where more capable systems begin.

  - Ideological commitment to LEAP as the sole navigation primitive
    failed both ends of the user spectrum.

- Undo (footnote candidate)

  - The keyboard had a dedicated Undo key -- but per the manual, "You can't
    UNDO typing." Undoing text entry required pressing both LEAP keys plus a
    separate Erase key.
  - No edit history: only the last operation could be undone.
  - A minor point relative to the modes argument, but further evidence that
    the humane interface was less humane in practice than in theory.

- The theory-product gap

  - Taken together: the Cat didn't escape modifiers, smuggled in the modes it
    claimed to avoid, locked out beginners from the start, and couldn't
    generalize beyond a single domain.
  - The empirical mouse critique -- efficiency is measurable, not just felt
    -- survives these failures and remains Raskin's real contribution. The Cat
    itself is more useful as a cautionary case than as a model.

- Transition to S5

  - The Cat's failure at the design level is not just an embarrassment for a
    particular product. It is a clue. If Raskin -- the most committed
    anti-modes theorist in the field -- could not build a real system without
    modes, that is evidence that modes are not a design flaw to be engineered
    around. They may be a structural feature of how computing works.

## S5: Modes are inevitable -- the question is only how

- Transition in: the Cat as evidence, not just cautionary tale. If modes
  couldn't be avoided in a single-domain appliance designed by their most
  determined opponent, they almost certainly can't be avoided in
  general-purpose computing.

- Modes are everywhere

  - Applications are themselves modes. Pressing `x` in Excel does something
    different from pressing `x` in LoopLlama. Every time a user switches
    applications, they switch modes -- they just aren't labeled as such.
  - Dialog boxes, confirmation prompts, focus capture, application states:
    computing is saturated with context-dependent behavior. The question was
    never whether modes exist. It was always what properties make them
    cognitively manageable.
  - Raskin's anti-modes position, taken literally, is incoherent as an
    absolute claim. A modeless general-purpose computer is not possible; it
    would require a single flat namespace for every command in every
    application, with no context-sensitivity of any kind.

- What makes a mode good or bad: five criteria (our framework, not sourced
  from Raskin or a specific text -- derived from the argument)

  - Visibility: is the current mode indicated to the user? The user should
    never have to guess.
  - Switching cost: is the transition deliberate and low-cost? Accidental
    mode changes -- the Caps Lock problem -- are the failure case.
  - Task clarity: does the mode correspond to a task distinction the user
    already recognizes? A mode that maps to a real phase of work (navigating
    vs. entering text) costs less than one that maps to an arbitrary
    technical state.
  - Coherence: do the behaviors grouped under the mode share a logical
    identity, and does the mode stay bounded -- no unexpected leakage into
    other territory?
  - Utility proportionality: does the real estate and activation prominence
    invested in this mode match the frequency and importance of the task it
    serves? A rare task with a cheap workaround does not earn a dedicated
    prime-real-estate key.

- A note on mode overuse

  - The same framework that rescues well-designed modes also condemns
    mode-heavy designs that fail proportionality across the board. A
    mode-fanatic who adds modes for every minor task -- plausible, given how
    satisfying the namespace expansion feels -- produces a different kind of
    complexity: many small namespaces, each requiring a mental context shift,
    few of them earning their keep by the proportionality criterion.
  - The framework is a test, not a license. Modes are justified when they
    pass all five criteria; they are not justified simply because they can
    be added.

- Two examples against the criteria

  - Caps Lock: fails on three of five. No reliable visual indicator
    (visibility). Accidental activation via prime real estate with minimal
    friction (switching cost -- the problem is not that switching is hard but
    that it happens when you didn't intend it). And the task -- typing in all
    capitals -- is rare, has a cheap modern workaround (type and recase), and
    occupies a permanently dedicated key on the home row (utility
    proportionality). Task clarity and coherence are fine; all-caps is a
    legitimate and coherent category. The design failures are elsewhere.

  - Vim insert/normal: passes most criteria, with caveats. The mode
    distinction maps to a real task difference (structural navigation vs.
    direct text entry) with genuine coherence. Switching cost is low and
    deliberate. The weakness is visibility: vim's default mode indicator is
    minimal, and users do lose track. The vim advocate's response -- that the
    tax rate drops to near zero once the modes are internalized and
    because vim's undo system is so powerful/easy to use -- is true but
    doesn't excuse the weak default. A better mode indicator could
    have been built into vim from the outset.

- The jujitsu: Raskin's automaticity argument supports good modes

  - Raskin's strongest argument against modes is about automaticity: the
    state where a practiced skill executes without conscious attention. Modes
    prevent automaticity from forming reliably, or cause it to misfire -- the
    mode error, where a practiced habit fires in the wrong context.
  - This is correct as a description of *bad* modes. But look at what the
    argument actually requires: consistency and visible state. Those are
    exactly the first two criteria for a good mode.
  - A mode that satisfies the four criteria gives automaticity a stable
    surface to form on. The user learns one behavior set for navigation, a
    different one for text entry. Neither interferes with the other because
    transitions are deliberate and visible. No mode errors; no invisible
    state; no inconsistency.
  - Raskin correctly diagnosed what makes modes fail. He drew the wrong
    conclusion. The solution to bad modes is not the absence of modes -- it
    is modes designed to the criteria his own argument implies.

- Transition to S6

  - Modes, done well, solve one problem: they expand the available command
    namespace without modifiers, by making context do the work that held keys
    would otherwise do. But solving the namespace problem is only half the
    job. Within any mode, you still need a command vocabulary a user can
    actually learn and remember. That is a separate problem -- and it is
    where the modifier tradition, even at its best, has always fallen short.

## S6: Grammar and multi-key bindings

- Transition in: pick up the planted thread from S2. Modifiers solve
  real estate but destroy memorability. Within a well-designed mode, the
  same problem recurs: how do you give a user hundreds of commands they
  can actually retain? The answer is grammar.

- What grammar means here

  - Not natural language grammar -- a narrower claim: a small set of
    combinatorial rules that generates a large command vocabulary, where the
    meaning of any combination is derivable from the meaning of its parts.
  - Structure does the memorization work. You don't memorize each binding as
    an atom; you learn the components and their combinations follow.
  - Vim as the canonical example:
    - VERB: an operation (insert, append, delete, yank/copy, change)
    - NOUN/MOTION: a text object (char, word, line, paragraph, sentence) or
      a range defined by a motion (2 paragraphs, 3 words, to bottom of screen)
    - QUANTIFIER: a number prefixing the verb, noun, or both
    - `d3w` is not memorized as a unit. You know `d` (delete), `3` (count),
      `w` (word). The combination is inferrable.
    - A small vocabulary of components generates a very large command space.

- The real-estate math (development of the S2 planted point)

  - Simplified model: 35 keys (letters plus common punctuation), one
    modifier (SHIFT).
  - Modifier approach: 35 × 2 = 70 bindings. Each key participates in
    at most two commands.
  - Multi-key sequential approach: 35 × 35 = 1,225 two-key combinations.
    Three-key sequences multiply further.
  - The quantity argument matters, but the mnemonic-space argument matters
    more: in the modifier scheme, each key is locked into one or two
    arbitrary assignments. In the multi-key scheme, each key can serve as
    a prefix for a whole family of related commands -- `v` for all
    video-related operations, `c` for all chapter-related operations. The
    namespace is not just larger; it is organizable by meaning.
  - Ergonomic bonus: sequential keypresses require no simultaneous
    hand contortion. Each key is pressed and released before the next.

- Cognitive science backing

  - Depth-of-processing theory: semantically encoded material -- meaning
    attached, connected to existing knowledge -- is retained far better
    than rote symbol-to-action pairs. A binding you understand why you
    pressed sticks; one you merely memorized fades.
  - Chunking and schema: the cognitive load of a grammatical binding
    system is not proportional to the number of commands. You learn the
    rules; the combinations are derivable. The schema does the
    memorization work.
  - Natural language analogy: every fluent speaker already operates a
    grammatical system orders of magnitude more complex than any
    application's command vocabulary. The cognitive machinery is there;
    grammar-based bindings plug into it. Modifier-based bindings do not.

- Modes as the outer grammar layer

  - A mode is the outermost namespace: it determines which grammar
    applies. The full sequence is [mode] [noun] [verb].
  - Applications add another outer layer: [app] [mode] [noun] [verb].
  - Modes are what let the grammar scale to large applications without
    collision. Without modes, all commands must share a single namespace;
    the grammar can only grow so far before prefixes conflict. Modes give
    each task domain its own grammar space.

- LoopLlama as the worked example

  - LL's binding scheme is the concrete instance of everything argued above.
    This is where the essay's occasion and its argument converge.
  - Entity vocabulary, mostly mnemonic: v = video, c = chapter, s = section, l
    = loop, m = mark, j = jump, d = delete. A new user seeing `vd` can make a
    reasonable guess.
  - The most common operations get double-letter bindings, because those are
    the easiest to type. In LL, those operations are creating new entities
    (cc, ss, ll, mm), setting scratch bounds (`[[` and `]]`), and toggling
    looping (xx) to give a few examples.
  - One non-mnemonic exception: `s` was claimed by sections, so the scratch
    loop family uses `x` as its prefix. As long as exceptions are limited in
    number, the overall scheme remains learnable. The grammar carries the
    load; isolated exceptions don't break it.
  - Which-key as the discoverability mechanism: press any prefix and the
    available completions appear. Education at the moment of motivated need --
    the user is already reaching for the command when the hint appears. This
    is the scalable version of what the Cat tried to accomplish with printed
    key labels: contextual, just-in-time, not limited to a fixed domain.
  - The cognitive demands of learning LL's binding system are lower than the
    demands of learning LL's features -- which the user has to learn
    regardless. The bindings come nearly for free once the vocabulary is
    established.

- Transition to S7

  - Modes plus grammar is the answer Raskin was looking for: keyboard
    efficiency for everyone, not just experts, without custom hardware
    and without a fixed-domain appliance. The approach generalizes. The
    question the conclusion takes up is why, if this is available, the
    ecosystem didn't build that way -- and what it would take for it to.

## S7: The ecosystem problem

- Transition in: the argument is complete on the technical level. The
  remaining question is structural: why, if this approach is available
  and demonstrably superior, did the ecosystem not converge on it?

- The path of least resistance

  - For any developer building keyboard support, the default is to
    inherit OS modifier conventions. The platform supplies them; users
    recognize them; tooling expects them. Doing something else requires
    deliberate effort against the grain.
  - Developer culture, OS APIs, user expectations, and platform
    documentation all reinforce modifier-based schemes generationally.
    Each layer of the ecosystem reflects and reproduces the choices made
    by the layer above it.
  - This is not negligence or conspiracy. It is what happens when
    infrastructure is built one way and culture follows. The path of
    least resistance leads to menus and modifiers, and most developers
    take it.

- The developer who never decided

  - Most applications with keyboard support have a modifier-based shortcut
    list accessible from the preferences menu. The developers who built
    them did not make a considered choice to reject grammar-based bindings.
    The platform offered modifiers; the tooling supported modifiers; the
    documentation showed modifiers. They followed the path. The outcome
    was determined before they started.

- The stagnation (NOTE: consider whether/where to make this point)

  - The early period of personal computing was genuinely exploratory:
    Engelbart's NLS, the Canon Cat, Smalltalk, various hardware
    experiments. People were actively trying to think about better
    ways to control computers.
  - The Mac/Windows paradigm won commercially and the book closed --
    fast, and almost completely. The major OS makers have cranked out
    new versions for decades with minimal change to the fundamental
    input paradigm. The Cat, for all its flaws, was at least trying
    something different.
  - The paradigm is self-reinforcing: users have internalized it so
    thoroughly that departures feel broken rather than better. (Win8
    as an example -- wrong direction, but illustrates how hard the
    ecosystem resists change even when someone tries.)
  - The tragedy is not only that good systems are the hard path -- it
    is also that the window for rethinking may have closed. The
    infrastructure, culture, and user expectations now all point the
    same way.

- Vim and LoopLlama swim upstream

  - Individual counter-examples prove the approach works. Vim is the
    canonical case: decades of sustained use across millions of users,
    despite (or because of) a grammar-based binding system that the
    ecosystem offered no help in building.
  - LoopLlama is a smaller case: a single-developer tool that chose the
    harder path and got a more learnable, more memorable binding system
    as a result.
  - But both are exceptions maintained by people who chose to swim
    against the current. The ecosystem sets the median outcome, not the
    exceptions. Requiring exceptional deliberateness from every developer
    to produce adequate UX is not a solution; it is a description of
    the problem.

- The counterfactual

  - If the major OS makers had built culture and infrastructure oriented
    around structured, namespace-based binding systems -- grammar-based
    binding toolkits, which-key as a platform primitive, documentation
    standards for hierarchical schemes -- the path of least resistance
    would lead somewhere better.
  - Even developers who never think carefully about UX would produce
    better defaults by following the platform. The median outcome would
    shift without requiring every developer to be exceptional.
  - The tragedy is not that good systems are impossible or even difficult
    to design. It is that the ecosystem was built in a way that makes
    them the hard path -- and then reproduced that structure across
    decades of software.

- Closing

  - Return to LoopLlama: building it made the argument concrete. The
    hard path is legible once you've walked it; the decisions that
    produced the current ecosystem become visible; and the shape of a
    better one becomes easier to describe.
  - The essay's argument is not only a diagnosis. It is a description
    of what right looks like -- grammar, modes, multi-key hierarchy,
    which-key discoverability. The pieces exist. They are just not the
    default.
