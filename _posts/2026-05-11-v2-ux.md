All software faces a basic question: how does the user tell it what to do?
Since the 1980s the dominant model has been drearily consistent: reach for the
mouse, navigate into an application's menus, and click the needed operation.
Ordinary navigational tasks are similar, minus the menus: reach, point,
scroll, click. Keyboard shortcuts for some of those operations exist. Nearly
all users learn a handful — copy, paste, print, arrow keys — and stop there.
Some users invest real effort to learn the bindings of applications they use
heavily, but even the office expert in Excel eventually hits a ceiling: the
brain will hold only so many arbitrary `Ctrl-Alt` combinations.

I recently completed [LoopLlama][llv2], a browser-based tool for close study
of YouTube videos. The application has menus and familiar mouse-oriented
controls such as buttons, dropdowns, and toggles. But at its core, LoopLlama
has a keyboard-first design: my goal was to control everything with simple key
presses while holding a guitar and wearing a thumb pick, without tedious
pointing and clicking. Building it sharpened my convictions about keyboard
design and produced a working example of a better UX paradigm — one with
strong historical precedents that computing culture ignored as it solidified
in the late twentieth century.

## The trouble with mice

The mouse is an instrument tuned for spatial actions: pointing, dragging,
positioning, drawing — tasks with a direct mapping between hand movement and
cursor position.

Command input is not that. When a user reaches for the mouse to invoke a menu
item or to navigate, nothing about that operation requires pointing. The
action is discrete: the user wants to save a file, undo a change, or toggle a
setting. The mouse arrives at the target by the same mechanism it uses to draw
a curve — slow physical travel, target acquisition, and click — but the
curve-drawing precision is wasted. You do not need a pointing instrument to
say *save*. Like a cartoon caveman, you point for routine actions only because
you lack the words.

The costs are individually tiny, but across a day of mousing the ergonomic and
time tax adds up. Less obviously but more importantly, mouse operations are
not readily chained or scripted. The keyboard, by contrast, is already where
the hands are, and its input is discrete, precise, and directly amenable to
composition into larger, automatable operations.

The case for the keyboard as the natural command instrument is
straightforward, and the problem was recognized early in computing history.
Two examples will suffice:

  - [Douglas Engelbart][douglas_engelbart] led the Augmentation Research
    Center in SRI International during the 1960s. In 1968, Engelbart and other
    ARC staffers presented their [oN-Line System][nls] (NLS), which introduced
    many of the elements of modern, interactive computing, including the
    computer mouse. The presentation was so striking to subsequent observers
    that it became known as [The Mother of All Demos][mother_demos]. Aiming to
    make knowledge work more powerful, Engelbart and his team understood the
    division of labor sketched above. NLS paired the mouse with the keyboard:
    the mouse for spatial tasks, the keyboard for text entry and commands.[^1]
    Only the pointing half of that vision survived contact with the
    marketplace.

  - Later in the 1980s and 1990s, human-computer interface expert [Jef
    Raskin][jef_raskin] put the case in more rigorous terms, drawing on
    empirical models of human motor performance and task completion time.[^2]
    Applied to command input, they confirmed and quantified the argument:
    mouse operations are slower than keyboard equivalents for discrete
    commands.

## The command-line and its constraints

Although the consumer market would become dominated by mouse-and-menus, an
older tradition — Unix and the command-line world — had built a culture around
keyboard primacy. Since command-line computing became powerful before the
mouse existed as a widely available device, the mouse could, at best, augment
it at the margins.

The applications running in that computing tradition faced an unavoidable
constraint: their large command vocabularies exceeded the keyboard's limited
real estate. A typical modern keyboard has about 50 keys producing regular
characters, and only 26 of them (the letters) have obvious mnemonic
potential.[^3] LoopLlama, for example, is a rich application for its purpose,
but it is tiny compared to the software that computer users spend the most
time with: word processors, spreadsheet applications, web browsers, and many
others. LoopLlama has roughly 100 bindings to cover its operations — already
twice the keyboard's raw capacity.

The dominant answer to that constraint was keyboard expansion, along two
pathways. One was additive: function keys, a dedicated row of extra keys above
the main keyboard, provided twelve new binding slots.

The second was more powerful and, at least in principle, multiplicative:
modifier keys. A keyboard's default behavior is to emit a character on each
keypress; a modifier intercepts that signal and redirects it, allowing the
same key to serve double or triple duty. As modifiers accumulated over time
— `CTRL` in early terminals, `Alt` from the IBM PC, `Cmd` and `Option` from
the Mac — the available binding space grew accordingly.

By the end of the 1990s, a keyboard-oriented user would have not only more
keys — function keys, arrows, a navigational cluster, and a number keypad
— but also several modifiers. The net effect was substantial: applications
running in those environments had hundreds of available binding slots, roughly
800 by my back-of-the-envelope calculation.

Although the expansion of key binding real estate was impressive in raw
numbers, most of that terrain turned out to be useless in practice, because
humans cannot remember the bindings. The letter component of a binding can
carry genuine meaning: `Cmd-p` for print, `Cmd-s` for save, `Cmd-c` for copy.
But the modifiers themselves are abstractions. Any given operation might use
`Cmd`, `Cmd-Shift`, `Cmd-Option`, or something more elaborate. Under favorable
conditions, a well-designed application could group bindings thematically
under different modifier combinations to reduce the mnemonic burden. In
practice, the logic behind the binding schemes of many major applications is
somewhere between opaque and non-existent.

The other problem with the modifier strategy is physical. Although `Shift`
sits at the natural extension of the pinky's reach, the primary modifiers for
issuing commands are ergonomic disasters, involving either awkward stretches
to reach a single modifier or full shifts out of typing position to press more
than one.

In the consumer market, a third pathway emerged: for many operations, there
would simply be no key binding. Most users learned a handful of keyboard
shortcuts and stopped there. The rest of their command vocabulary lived behind
menus and long days of point and click.

One cultural response to those constraints was a call to embrace expertise —
to stop complaining about the difficulty of keyboard mastery and invest the
effort to achieve it. No one made that case more vividly than Neal Stephenson
in [In the Beginning... Was the Command Line][stephenson_essay]: Linux as a
freely available tank, Windows as a breakdown-prone station wagon, and Mac as
an elegant but confining sports car. Stephenson understood why so many users
opted for the initial ease and reassurance of purchasing from a friendly
dealer, but he upheld the virtues of the few who would invest the time to
learn how to drive and maintain a tank.

Stephenson appeals to a minority willing to commit to an entire computing
paradigm. A different question about expertise applies more broadly. Because
their needs do not justify it, most people will never invest that heavily in
most of the software they use. But most people have at least one application
they use often enough to warrant such investment: the person who spends a
significant chunk of every workday in Word, Excel, or Outlook; the retiree who
manages photos in Lightroom every weekend; the teacher who assembles lecture
slides in PowerPoint. Additional expertise would pay off. But if the route to
expertise goes through a thicket of modifier-based key bindings, few will make
the journey.

## Raskin's case against modes

Interface expert Jef Raskin had long criticized both computing paradigms on
offer. On one pole was the expert-oriented computing tradition of Unix and the
command-line. Although powerful, this user interface provided poor visual
feedback and was too demanding for ordinary computer users. In any case, since
it had already lost any claim on the mainstream, it was not his primary
target. Meanwhile, the GUI systems from Microsoft and Apple had become almost
as opaque as the command-line systems they replaced, with deep menu
hierarchies that required users to know where to look when trying to perform a
task — a mnemonic burden in a new form.

In 2000 Raskin published [The Humane Interface][humane_interface], which
brought together critiques he had been developing since the 1980s. The book's
guiding claims were that interfaces should prioritize alignment with human
cognition rather than computer internals and that design should aim to
increase efficiency for all users, not just dedicated experts.

Both cognitive science and everyday experience show that practiced actions
become automatic: driving, typing, using a TV remote, riding a bicycle. A
central idea in Raskin's work is that good interfaces should aim to support
automaticity — for users to stop consciously deciding and simply to act. By
contrast, interfaces that change from version to version; that have different
behaviors in different contexts, forcing users to track state mentally; that
have a lack of feedback or large delays between action and response; that have
high error costs (for example, no undo), forcing users into hyper-diligence —
such traits force users to act consciously rather than automatically.

Raskin identified modes as the most systematic of these failures. A mode is a
state of the interface in which the same user action produces different
results. The computing environment of the 1980s and 1990s supplied vivid
examples: the Caps Lock key, inherited from the typewriter, which silently
shifted every subsequent keystroke to uppercase; word processors that toggled
between inserting and overwriting text; image editors and presentation tools
that required the user to track which drawing tool was active; and vi, the
command-line world's dominant text editor, built on a modal foundation. In
each case, the same action — a key press or a mouse click — produced different
results depending on application state that the user had to remember.

In such interfaces, mode errors can occur any time the user becomes too
preoccupied or rushed and forgets which mode is active before typing or
clicking. Such errors were bothersome for novice users, but Raskin's deeper
critique emphasized how they undermined the automaticity upon which expertise
rested. The same automaticity that makes experts efficient makes them
particularly vulnerable to mode errors: if you wrongly think you are in
insert-mode in vi, the more letters you type, the more likely you are to do
some damage.

Raskin's prescription followed from the diagnosis: modes should be eliminated
whenever possible. Where they could not be, his preferred alternative was the
quasimode — a held key, active only while pressed. Unlike a persistent mode, a
quasimode cannot be easily forgotten because it is tactile.

## The Canon Cat: theory meets practice

Although Raskin had played a leading role in an early phase of the Macintosh
project, he was disappointed by the result — too mouse-heavy, too dependent on
menus, too many modal interruptions. In 1987 he led the design of an
alternative: the [Canon Cat][wiki_canon_cat], a word-processing computer that
provided an early working example of the [interface principles][cat_promo] he
would later codify in *The Humane Interface*.

The Cat's design consciously rejected two of the three prevailing strategies
for solving the keyboard real estate problem. The surrender strategy — putting
lower-priority operations behind menus and requiring the user to point and
click for anything without a binding — was unacceptable on the terms Raskin
had already established. Another strategy — embracing modes more fully —
addresses real estate by allowing the same keys to serve different functions
depending on the interface's current state. That too was off the table for
Raskin, since modes were the primary target of his critique. That left
keyboard expansion, specifically the modifier approach, as the only viable
path. The [Cat innovated][cat_keyboard] on that approach in two ways.

The first was a pair of `USE FRONT` keys, positioned on the left and right
sides of the spacebar — replacing the usual modifier keys in that position.
The `USE FRONT` keys activated a quasimode: hold the key and the keyboard's
behavior changed. The payload was printed on the front edges of the keys
themselves — Copy, Calc, Print, Bold, Indent, Spell Checker, and so on. To
invoke an operation, the user pressed and held `USE FRONT` with one thumb and
pressed the labeled key with a finger. The operation catalog was visible on
the hardware, eliminating recall as a prerequisite for access.

The second innovation was a pair of `LEAP` keys, positioned below the spacebar
for thumb operation — a significant ergnomic improvement relative to the
placement of the usual modifier keys. `LEAP` was the Cat's primary navigation
primitive. Holding `LEAP` while typing a literal string moved the cursor
dynamically through the document as each character was entered — in effect, an
incremental search. The same mechanism supported text selection: position the
cursor at one endpoint, then `LEAP` to the other; pressing both `LEAP` keys
simultaneously highlighted the text between them. Like `USE FRONT`, the `LEAP`
keys were quasimodal, active only while held, so their effect on the
keyboard's behavior was always explicit and tactile.

The Cat sold poorly and was discontinued after [six months][cat_six_months] on
the market. That failure could be attributed to business strategy or the
vagaries of a fluid computing market in 1987 — plausible enough explanations.
My retrospective judgment, though, is that the Cat was built on a deeply
flawed vision, and that the flaws were systematic rather than incidental. They
cluster around three aspects of the design.

The first flaw is that the Cat does, in fact, adopt the surrender strategy —
just in a different form than the mouse-and-menu world did. The GUI market
surrendered by exiling lower-priority operations to menus, forcing users to
reach for the mouse whenever they needed something without a binding. The Cat
avoided that move, but only by drastically restricting what the device was
expected to do. At its core, it was a word-processing appliance, not a
general-purpose computer. It had computing at the margins — a calculator
accessible via the CALC key, and a lower-level toolchain for Forth and
assembly — but it had no support for third-party software. The operation
catalog could fit on the front edges of the keys precisely because the domain
had been narrowed.

The second flaw is that `LEAP`, as the Cat's sole navigation primitive, failed
both ends of the user spectrum, leaving it in an interface sour spot:

- For beginners, the Cat was a [RTFM][wiki_rtfm] device from the start. It had
  no arrow keys. The `LEAP` keys could move the cursor one character at a time
  (called "creeping"), but the manual admonishes users not to rely on creeping
  as a crutch. Once a user did learn to leap, the physical tax was permanent:
  every navigation required holding `LEAP` while typing a search string. That
  is the inescapable cost of the quasimode approach — the same cost paid by
  every modifier scheme, but magnified to include entire query strings.
  Finally, although the promotional video made the Cat look effortless, for
  less frequent operations the user manual told a different story: the
  instructions took real effort to decipher, and the key sequences seemed
  destined to remain balky.

- Intermediate and expert users hit low ceiling. Leaping was literal text
  search: no wildcards, patterns, regex, or text objects. GUI word processors
  had long offered at least wildcards and navigational support for words and
  paragraphs. Command-line editors went much farther on both fronts. The best
  the user manual could offer were approximate literal-search workarounds for
  some text objects.

The third flaw cuts deepest, because it is a theory violation, not just a
limit. The Cat's cursor had two states — wide and narrow — that controlled the
direction of text erasure and whether typing would insert or replace existing
text. By Raskin's own definition, this is a mode. More striking still: the
same behavior is found in vi, the command-line text editor proudly standing on
the opposite side of the modes debate. Vi users navigate among normal mode,
insert mode, and replace mode — distinctions conveyed via cursor shape, just
like the Cat. The most determined opponent of modes in computing built them
into his signature product.

## There will be modes

The Cat's cursor mode is a non-trivial violation of Raskin's own theory and a
clue — a suggestion that wisdom lies in the recognition that modes are
unavoidable.

<span class="phead">Applications required</span>. The logical case is direct
and begins with the inevitability of applications. To modern computer users,
that is a puzzling place to start — a claim so obvious as to be hardly worth
making. But Raskin's writing and the Cat's advertising material sometimes
conveyed the idea that computer users should not have to bother with pesky
details like applications, desktops, directories, and even files. At the same
time, however, a [promotional video][cat_promo] for the Cat explicitly
mentions spreadsheets and database applications running on the device. In some
sense, the admission was compelled because, by 1987, such applications were
well on their way to becoming standard in office computing. The video glosses
over the apparent contradiction — was the Cat an appliance optimized for
editing text or a general-purpose computer? — by showing only toy examples:
small spreadsheet tables or data-editing forms embedded within a larger text
document. Anyone who has spent serious time in a spreadsheet application knows
how fanciful that vision is. A spreadsheet is not a word processor, and a
database application is not a spreadsheet. Each involves different data,
different tasks, and thus different interfaces. Cramming Excel into a text
editor will not cut it.

<span class="phead">Applications are modes</span>. Each application's distinct
interface is, by definition, a mode: a state in which the same keyboard and
mouse inputs produce different results. To grant that different applications
require their own interfaces — and there is no plausible argument against this
— is to grant that some modes are unavoidable.

<span class="phead">Rinse and repeat: modes within applications</span>. The
same reasoning applies at the next level down. Nearly all non-trivial
applications need to support multiple kinds of work. Mapping applications have
searching and exploring, but also direct navigation to a specific destination.
Presentation software has the task of creating slides as well as presenting
them. More directly relevant for our purposes, many applications — notably,
word processors, text editors, and spreadsheets — need to support at least two
types of work: directly entering literal text versus issuing various commands
for editing and navigation. In order to be effective and efficient, the
interfaces for the different tasks will necessarily be different: the bigger
the task difference, the bigger the interface difference. Modes necessarily
enter the scene.

<span class="phead">Refutation by counterexample: QED</span>. If computing
operates with at least two irreducible layers of modes — applications and then
major tasks interfaces within them — a blanket prohibition cannot stand. In
fairness, Raskin's views were not that rigid, but he did make strong claims
about the harmfulness of modes and his interest in eliminating them to the
extent possible. At this point, however, a strong anti-modes posture has lost
its confident footing: the debate is no longer whether modes are harmful or
should exist; the terrain necessarily shifts to sharper questions.

The relevent questions deal with the characteristics that make modes
beneficial, cognitively manageable, and worth having. Four seem most relevant:

  - Visibility. A mode the user cannot readily see is on the user will forget,
    leading to errors. A clear indicator keeps the current mode in peripheral
    awareness without demanding attention.

  - Task clarity. The mode should correspond to a phase of work the user
    recognizes as meaningfully distinct. A mode organized around a genuine
    task distinction gives users a natural mental model: they know which mode
    they should be in because they know what they are doing.

  - Switching cost-benefit. The transition into and out of the mode should be
    deliberate and low-cost — easy enough that users do not avoid it, bounded
    enough that accidental activation is unlikely. In addition, the key or
    gesture used to enter the mode occupies real estate that could serve other
    purposes, and that expenditure should be proportionate to the mode's value
    in supporting useful work.

  - Risk. When the user acts in the wrong mode, what happens? Some errors are
    trivially reversible; others can destroy work. The severity of potential
    mode errors is part of the cost-benefit ledger.

Two examples show how these criteria play out in practice:

  - Caps Lock fails on most of them. Its indicator — a small LED on most
    keyboards — is easy to overlook, particularly on laptops. Accidental
    activation is common: the key occupies prime real estate at the left edge
    of the home row, where an errant pinky routinely lands. And the task it
    serves — sustained all-caps typing — is infrequent for most users, with
    cheap workarounds available in any modern editor. Valable keyboard real
    estate, easy accidental activation, low-frequency task: the cost-benefit
    is badly skewed.

  - Vi's insert and normal modes tell the other story. The task distinction
    is genuine — one mode for entering literal text, one for navigation and
    editing commands — and users learn it readily because it tracks something
    real about the work. Switching cost is low: a single keypress in each
    direction. The risk from mode errors is real but substantially mitigated
    by vi's strong undo system. The main shortcoming is visibility: a
    cursor-shape change, the same mechanism the Cat used — a real but subtle
    mechanism that is arguably not as prominent as it could be, especially on
    modern computer displays. Nonetheless, on balance, these modes earn their
    place.

Raskin's core concern about modes was automaticity: that users would have to
maintain conscious awareness of the current mode state, and that this overhead
would prevent frequent actions from becoming practiced habits. The concern is
real for poorly designed modes, but it gets the relationship backward for
well-designed ones. A mode organized around a genuine task distinction does
not burden the user's cognitive apparatus — it becomes part of it. In vi,
normal mode is for moving around and manipulating text; insert mode is for
typing. Neither interferes with the other because the system's organization is
sensible, intuitive. The modes become an organizing hook — similar to the
conceptual order supplied at a higher level by applications — and thus become
a stable and meaningful base upon which automaticity can form.

## Modes all the way down

Accepting that modes are inevitable, and that well-designed ones support
rather than undermine automaticity, opens a further opportunity: modes can
become a direct solution to the keyboard real estate problem that drove
computing toward modifiers and menus in the first place.

That problem was a mismatch between command vocabulary size and available
binding slots, producing the strategies already described: more modifier
combinations, function keys, surrender to menus. All of them shared a hidden
assumption: that the keyboard's default job is to emit characters, so bindings
must be carved out against that baseline.

A dedicated typing mode dissolves the assumption. Once the application has a
specific mode for literal text entry, every other mode is free. The letters,
digits, and punctuation marks become binding real estate — no modifier
required, no awkward reaches. Every key, especially the letters, have the
potential to carry meaning directly (`d` for delete, `w` for word). None of
the keys are pure abstractions the way the modifiers are. A typing mode is an
architectural move that restructures the real estate problem entirely.

That key and mnemonic expansion is the first step. The second adds structure
to the binding scheme — further expanding available command space, while
making it more meaningful and memorable. The normal-mode bindings of vi are
illustrative. That command space is not a flat list of bindings to be
memorized; it is built from a small vocabulary of atoms governed by a grammar,
and the grammar generates operations combinatorially.

The atoms come in three roles. Verbs express operations: `d` for delete, `y`
for yank (copy), `c` for change. Nouns name text objects or navigation
targets: `w` for word start, `ap` for a paragraph including its following
blank lines, `ip` for the paragraph's inner text. Quantifiers are numeric
prefixes that scale the verb, the noun, or both. Users learn those atoms, and
the grammar then makes their combinations largely derivable: `dw` deletes a
word; `d5w` deletes five; `yap` yanks the current paragraph. Some verbs can
act as stand-alone imperatives — `i` to enter insert mode, `p` to paste — with
no noun required.

The cognitive arithmetic is favorable. A modest vocabulary of the most useful
verbs and nouns can generate a large variety of commands. The grammar's
structure does most of the memorization work.

A typing mode creates a generous supply of letter-based real estate, but a
rich application will outrun single-key capacity. LoopLlama has roughly a
hundred bindings; a text editor has far more. The same structural principle
that grammar applies to atoms can be applied one level down: use the first key
of a sequence not as a complete operation but as a prefix — a kind of mode —
that organizes a family of related commands.

The arithmetic is striking. A modifier strategy starting with 26 letters
and adding `Shift` yields 52 binding slots. A multi-key strategy using the
same 26 letters as two-character sequences yields 676. But the raw count
understates the advantage, because the two approaches differ not just in
quantity but in mnemonic quality. In a modifier scheme, the letters carry
meaning but the modifiers do not — they are pure abstractions. In a
multi-key scheme, both keys can carry meaning: the first key is a prefix
that organizes a family of related commands; the second identifies the
specific operation within that family.

A multi-key scheme dominates a modifier scheme not just quantitatively but
also ergonomically. Every modifier binding requires two key presses, the same
as a two-key multi-key binding. But the latter requires no awkward stretches,
no hand shifts out of typing position, so simultaneous presses.

Finally, such binding systems have a deeper advantage. Every fluent speaker
already operates a grammatical system orders of magnitude more complex than
any application's command vocabulary. The cognitive machinery for structured,
rule-governed recall is not exotic; it is among the most practiced
capabilities humans have. Multi-key, grammar-based bindings plug into that
machinery. Modifier-based bindings do not — they are arbitrary pairings the
brain must hold by rote.

## The Llama

LoopLlama is built on that architecture: a modal keyboard, a vocabulary of
meaningful prefix keys, and multi-key bindings organized according to that
vocabulary.

<span class="phead">Mnemonic atoms</span>. The grammar's elements are directly
mnemonic whenever possible. The language has nouns, such as `v` for video, `c`
for chapter, `s` for section, or `m` for mark. And it has verbs like `e` for
edit, `j` for jump, `d` for delete, or `z` for zoom.

<span class="phead">Noun then verb</span>. Those elements can be combined in
the same way that English speakers expect: noun then verb. For example, `ve`
to edit the current video, `mj` to jump to a mark, or `sd` to delete a
section.

<span class="phead">Doubles are prime real estate</span>. The most frequent
operations get the double-letter bindings — the easiest sequence to type. For
example, creating a new entity uses its prefix key doubled: `cc` for chapter,
`ss` for section, `ll` for loop, `mm` for mark.

<span class="phead">Mnemonic exceptions, thoughfully done</span>. As ever,
real estate is precious. For example, with `s` already claimed as a prefix by
sections, the scratch loop (the application's work area for looping) needed to
resort to a different prefix. The letter `x` was chosen, in part, because the
letter has the connotation of "scratch out". In that context, the most
frequent operation — toggling looping on and off — claims the `xx` binding.
Meanwhile, the scratch loop start and end points have their own binding needs
so the user can quickly adjust them in various ways. Each point has a prefix —
`[` for start and `]` for end — and the bracket pair carries the connotation
of an enclosed loop. The general principles are to use direct mnemonic devices
whenever feasible and to lean on indirect connections when necessary.
Exceptions of this kind are unavoidable in any real system and tolerable as
long as they remain limited in number. The grammar carries most of the
cognitive load; an isolated departure does not break it.

<span class="phead">Multi-key structure enables discoverability</span>. When
the user presses any binding prefix, a compact display of available
completions appears at the bottom of the screen. The user encounters the hint
at the moment of motivated need — already reaching for the command, not
browsing a reference page. This is the scalable version of what the Canon Cat
attempted with its printed key labels: contextual, just-in-time, and not
constrained by the fixed space of physical hardware.

The cognitive demands of learning LoopLlama's binding system are lower than
the demands of learning its features — which the user has to master
regardless. The bindings come nearly for free once the vocabulary is in place.

## The tragedy

A specific mode for direct typing, a vocabulary of nouns and verbs, and
multi-key sequences organized by grammar produce an input system that is more
capacious, learnable, memorable, and ergonomic than anything a paradigm
organized about mouse, menus, and modifiers can deliver. LoopLlama is evidence
that the approach can be taken all the way to its logical conclusions. Vi is
evidence that it can sustain millions of users across decades.

Perhaps it is not surprising that computing culture settled on a poor paradigm
for command input. That kind of suboptimal lock-in is common enough in the
history of technology. What converts the outcome from unfortunate to tragic is
how thoroughly experimentation stopped. The early period of personal computing
was genuinely exploratory. Engelbart's NLS paired mouse and keyboard on
principled lines. The [Unix philosophy][raymond_unix] of small, composable
tools showed keyboard-first computing at its most powerful — a system
architecture as much as an interface. Vi brought modal, grammar-based editing
to Unix in the late 1970s. Even the mouse-and-menus UX of the Mac — although a
prime target of this essay's critique — was truly innovative. In turn, Raskin
recognized the flaws of those mouse-centric systems and designed the Canon Cat
and developed his alternative ideas for *The Humane Interface* during the
1980s and 1990s. Those efforts had their flaws, but the field was alive to the
problem. Then the Windows and Mac operating systems won commercially, and the
exploration largely ended. The major platform makers have issued new versions
for decades with minimal change to the input model.

That stagnation the thing worth pausing on. Computing is the field most
associated with relentless change. Processing power, storage, networking,
displays, software distribution, and application domains have all been
transformed since the 1980s. The direct, tacile question of how the user tells
the computer what to do has not. A better approach was visible from the
beginning. The tragedy was that we looked away.

[^1]: NLS include both a regular keyboard and a [chorded
    keyboard][chorded_keyboard] — a five-key device held in the non-dominant
    hand — as a dedicated command interface to use while the mouse hand was
    occupied.

[^2]: Specifically, [Fitts's Law][fitts_law], which predicts target-acquisition
    time as a function of target distance and size, and the [Keystroke-Level
    Model][keystroke_model], which predicts task completion time from its
    physical components.

[^3]: The regular characters available for key bindings consist of 26 letters,
    10 numbers, and 11 punctuation, along with space, tab, and return. Other
    keys exist and can also be used for bindings (function keys, arrows, and
    various editing or navigational keys), but those vary more across hardware
    models.
