I did some more reading of various essays in the journalism/academic realm on
this subject and am concerned about framing for my post.

I was a legitimate economic and political historian ... a long time ago:
extensive reading/writing in the 1990s; lots of dissertation research and
policy-adjacent work experience in social sciences in the 2000s. But since
then I've mostly been a software engineer and, now, retired person working on
projects that interest me. I read a lot of media at the intersection of
journalism, policy analysis, popularized academic, and history, but I'm not
really "in the game" or up-to-date in the field. My expertise, to the extent I
had any, is old. Moreover, I was never a macroeconomic theorist working on
abstract topics like, "What happens if the economy becomes massively
productive due to highly capable AIs and AI-robots?"

The points I make in this essay about economic history are thoroghly routine:
everyone who understands the basics of this history knows and makes exactly
the same points I'm making, often citing the say stats (eg percent employed in
agriculture) and drawing the same conclusions (employment shifted rather than
collapsing).

So, what's the point of the essay? I sense that there can be one, but I need
to frame things carefully to avoid giving the impression that I'm somehow
delivering unknown truths. The truths are fully known regarding the history;
but that history tends to get underplayed in popular discussions. When
reviewing the econ history, I think I need to make those points very
efficiently (cut back on verbosity etc) and present them not as "findings" or
an illustration of my cleverness/knowledge, but more as a brief review of
well-worn knowledge that tends to get underplayed.

So if that part of the essay compresses and gets reframed a bit, is the rest
of the post saying enough to justify its existence? The LL v2 example might
also get compressed a lot, in the same spirit: "here's a concrete example and
it illustrates the same points". I could cover that quite compactly.

But after all of the necessary compression, does the post survive at all?

  - Cloud backup story:
